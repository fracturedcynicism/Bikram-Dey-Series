INJURY TIME

*A Novella*

**Fractured Cynicism**

*"The game is bigger than any one moment."*

*— Anonymous motivational poster, referee changing room,*

*Salt City Arena, repainted 1987, not repainted since*

*Injury time:*

*The minutes added at the end of a match for stoppages and incidents.*

*Liminal time. Time that should not exist but does.*

*Time in which anything can still happen.*

*And often, the worst things do.*

This story takes place in one night.

The match ends at ten-fifteen. The sun rises at five-fifty-two.

Everything happens in the space between those two facts.

**Chapter One**

# **The Three Sins**

*10:00 PM – 10:15 PM*

The first wrong call Bikram Dey made that evening was in the sixty-first minute, and it had nothing to do with football.

It had to do with his eyes.

He'd known for six weeks. The optometrist in Ultadanga had said it plainly, the way people in small rooms with large machines tend to say plain things: depth perception, moderate degradation, almost certainly nerve-related, strongly recommended he cease any activity requiring fine spatial judgement. Bikram had said thank you, folded the report into quarters, put it in his jacket pocket, and walked back to his guesthouse through a street that smelled of frying onions and diesel, where two boys were kicking a rubber ball against a wall with the focused intensity of prayer.

He'd kept the appointment with the Indian Football Federation on the grounds that what he didn't officially declare couldn't officially exclude him.

He had been wrong about that too, as it turned out.

— ◦ —

The match was a Bengal Shield qualifier. Crescent United FC versus Riverside Athletic FC. Salt City Arena. Night fixture, floodlights, forty thousand people in a stadium designed for forty-two. The stands ran from a low murmur to something closer to weather — you could feel it in the chest cavity. Crescent's supporters, the west stand packed from railing to railing, had been building noise since warm-up, and by the time the two captains met Bikram at the centre circle, the sound had texture.

Bikram Dey was not unacquainted with high-stakes environments. He'd spent four years in Manipur and two classified months in a situation he'd signed documents about that precluded him from discussing even with himself on certain evenings. He understood the particular quality of tension that precedes action. He'd been trained to read environments the way other men read text: rapidly and without effort, extracting signal from noise.

Football crowds were not ambushes in jungle terrain. He was still, four months into this posting, adjusting to that.

The match for fifty-seven minutes was exactly what the Bengal Shield needed it to be: physical, committed, marginally above adequate. Riverside played a defensive 4-4-2 with the resigned pragmatism of a team that understood they were the inferior side on the night. Crescent pressed high, circulated well, wasted three gilt-edged opportunities with the special profligacy of a home team playing in front of forty thousand people who expected better. The kind of chances that feel generous until you miss them.

Bikram moved. He tracked play. He managed the temperature — two yellow cards, both earned, both clean. His assistant referees were reliable. The first forty-five minutes and the initial stretch of the second half passed without incident, and there was a brief period around the hour mark when Bikram thought, with the measured optimism of a man who has been wrong before: perhaps tonight will be manageable.

Then came the sixty-first minute. And the penalty.

— ◦ —

It happened in the Riverside box. A Crescent forward — quick, aggressive, perpetually furious about perceived injustices — drove into the area and released a cross that dropped toward a defender's outstretched arm. Or appeared to. The ball struck something in that approximate region and rebounded. Bikram, twenty-six metres away and fractionally behind the line of play, saw a ball change direction off an outstretched limb and made the call that twenty-six metres and a degraded spatial processing system told him to make.

He pointed to the spot.

The wall of sound that followed was not applause.

It wasn't the Crescent fans who erupted. It was the Riverside contingent — six thousand people in the east stand who had watched their defender, on every available screen, take the ball cleanly on the shoulder. The shoulder. Under the laws of the game, the shoulder is not handball. Under the laws of the game, this was not a penalty. Under the laws of the game, Bikram had just given a forty-thousand-seat stadium irrefutable, replay-verified evidence that the man in the black shirt could not see properly.

Riverside converted.

1-0.

The stadium's silence — Crescent's silence — lasted precisely two seconds. Then it dissolved into its component parts: grief, rage, and the very specific sound of forty thousand people reaching simultaneously for their phones.

— ◦ —

Bikram did not look at the screens. He knew what the screens showed. He concentrated on his positioning, on the eighteen minutes that remained, on the maintenance of play.

The seventy-fourth minute produced the second call.

A Crescent forward received the ball in behind Riverside's defensive line and was — this was clear to everyone in the stadium, clear to every camera angle, clear in the immediate geometric facts of the situation — level. Exactly level. The line between onside and offside, at the professional level, is a matter of millimetres and certainty. Bikram had neither. His assistant raised the flag. Bikram confirmed. The goal was disallowed.

The replays began before he'd blown his whistle. Three journalists in the press box were filing copy. On the giant screens, the forward's shoulder was demonstrably, absurdly, level. Not offside. Level. The crowd's response was not grief this time. Grief had been spent. What replaced it was something older and more purposeful — the particular quality of anger that has found its object and is now organising itself around that object with quiet, furious intent.

1-0 still. Sixteen minutes remaining.

Then the eighty-ninth minute.

— ◦ —

Farrukh Tashkentov was nineteen years old, Uzbek international, the most gifted player on the pitch by a considerable distance — the sort of player who makes everyone else move like they're wading through something. He received the ball twenty-two metres out, drove left, and was brought down inside the Riverside box with the deliberate, cynical efficiency of a defender who understood that conceding a foul was preferable to conceding a goal. Studs. Knee height. Zero interest in the ball.

Bikram was out of position. He'd been tracking a run on the opposite flank. He came around with a partial view of the incident — enough to see the contact, not enough to read its nature. What he had was impression. Impression plus degraded spatial processing plus twenty-two metres plus the final minute of a high-stakes match in which he had already made two unforgivable calls.

He should have played on. He should have consulted his assistant. He should, by any reasonable professional standard, have let the moment breathe.

He reached for his yellow card.

He showed it to Tashkentov.

*Simulation.*

The boy stared at him. Not with rage — he was too young and too certain of what had happened to him for rage. He stared with the pure, unmediated bewilderment of someone confronted with a version of reality that contains no logical relationship to the reality they actually inhabit.

The stadium went very still.

Then the final whistle. Three minutes into injury time. Riverside Athletic win, 1-0. Crescent United are eliminated from the Bengal Shield.

Riverside's players celebrated with the subdued relief of men who know they have won something they should not have. Crescent's players were not celebrating anything. Farrukh Tashkentov sat on the pitch with his knees drawn to his chest and looked at the grass.

Forty thousand people were looking at Bikram Dey.

He began walking toward the tunnel.

**Chapter Two**

# **Civilian Clothes**

*10:15 PM – 10:47 PM*

The referee's changing room at Salt City Arena was a small rectangle painted institutional cream in 1987 and not repainted since. It contained three lockers, a wooden bench, a shower that produced water at one of two temperatures — scalding or ambient — and a motivational poster of uncertain origin that read: THE GAME IS BIGGER THAN ANY ONE MOMENT.

Bikram read it while he changed. He thought it was possible that the person who made that poster had not recently been responsible for three compounding, televised, replay-destroyed decisions that ended a team's season in front of forty thousand witnesses.

He undressed methodically. Folded the black referee's shirt. Placed it in his bag with the same care he'd once given to field equipment — the habit of orderly packing was older than almost anything else in him, older than the Army, older than football, probably older than most things he could name. Outside the changing room, he could hear the stadium disgorging itself. The specific sound of forty thousand people leaving a place where something terrible has happened: low, sustained, a sound like weather moving through a city it doesn't particularly like.

There was a knock. Aloke Bose, his assistant referee — young, mortified, clearly running internal calculations about the degree to which tonight's decisions were partially his failure and finding insufficient evidence to distribute the weight — came in and said that two constables from the stadium police detail were outside, ready to escort Bikram to the car.

Bikram thanked him.

He took a moment alone. He was not a man given to extended introspection — the Army had not trained him for it and the operational theatre actively discourages lingering in the space between action and its consequences. But the moment in the eighty-ninth minute, the yellow card, the boy's face, pressed against something behind his sternum with enough insistence to warrant brief acknowledgement.

He had made those calls. All three of them. From positions that were wrong, with eyes that were less than what the position required. He had been trained to know the difference between the limits of his competence and the demands of a situation. Tonight, across ninety-three minutes, he had known the difference and continued regardless.

He picked up his bag and went out.

— ◦ —

The car — a white Ambassador belonging nominally to the Bengal Football Association — was parked outside the stadium's service entrance on the eastern side, Gate 7. Bikram followed the two constables through the underbelly of the stadium, through corridors that smelled of concrete and old food and effort, past equipment rooms and a medical bay where a Riverside physio was writing up a report with the focused serenity of a man on the right side of a result.

Outside, the air was warm and diesel-thick. The city smelled of the end of an occasion. Vendors packing up. Two men arguing about something unrelated to football — or perhaps everything was related to football tonight; in this city, in this moment, he wasn't sure the categories were distinct.

Constable number one — young, slight, phone pressed to his ear since they'd left the changing room — stopped walking. His face changed in the specific way that faces change when personal news reorganises all professional priorities. He said: Aishwarya hospital-e admit hoyeche — admitted to hospital — and looked at his phone and then at Bikram with an expression that was simultaneously professional apology and absolute pre-emption.

His wife. Labour.

He was gone before Bikram could process the sentence.

Constable number two had been walking ahead. He reached Gate 7, found the Ambassador, opened the rear door, got in the front passenger seat, and pulled into the stream of vehicles leaving the service perimeter under the reasonable assumption that Bikram was thirty seconds behind him.

Bikram was thirty seconds behind him. The Ambassador was gone.

Bikram Dey, former Para Special Forces, twelve years' service, two operational deployments, decorated twice, stood alone on the service road outside Salt City Arena at ten forty-seven PM with his referee's bag, his phone at thirty-four percent battery, and his civilian clothes — grey trousers, a blue half-sleeve shirt he'd bought in New Market the week before, because he'd arrived in this city with insufficient shirts — and was temporarily uncertain what to do.

This was not a sensation he'd experienced often.

He took stock. Options: call someone, flag an auto, walk to Bidhan Nagar Road and find a taxi. His guesthouse was four kilometres away. His phone was at thirty-four percent. The stadium behind him was still releasing supporters, and the road, though wide, was not well lit near Gate 7.

He called Colonel Ghosh. No answer.

He called the IFF duty officer. No answer.

He looked at the road. Two groups of Crescent supporters were moving along the footpath forty metres ahead, scarves still around their necks, moving with the heavy gait of people who are processing something that will take weeks to fully process.

He decided to walk.

**Chapter Three**

# **Contact**

*10:47 PM – 11:12 PM*

He was spotted at the Bidhan Nagar Road crossing.

Eight hundred metres from Gate 7, keeping to the less-lit side of the road, he registered the signs: a group of six young men — Crescent scarves, voices carrying across the street with the hoarseness of people who have been shouting for ninety minutes — who slowed their collective walking and redirected their attention toward him with the precision of people whose instincts have just fired simultaneously.

Bikram performed a rapid threat assessment. Six individuals. Twenties, mostly. No visible weapons. Body language: agitated, energised, the post-match electricity not yet fully discharged. Not dangerous in a physical sense — not immediately. But the crowd physics of the situation were worse than six individuals. Six individuals with phones, in a city where everyone had just seen his face on the replays, was not six individuals. It was a network node.

The first one said: Oita shei referee na? — Is that the referee?

The second: Haan. Yes.

The third: Oi sala. That bastard.

Bikram kept walking. Don't stop. Don't engage. Create distance while the window is still open. He'd had a phrase for it in Manipur: move while the window is open. The window was open.

He increased pace.

The six increased pace.

*"Ei! Referee! **Daarao**!" — Hey! Stop!*

He did not stop.

He turned left onto a narrower lane — the kind that appears on no map and exists in this city the way capillaries exist in tissue, running through the body of the neighbourhood without announcement — and walked faster, and then he ran. Not a sprint. A measured, sustainable pace. Para SF conditioning is not designed for the hundred metres; it's designed for the twelve hours. He ran at the pace he could maintain while keeping spatial awareness: checking over his shoulder at intervals, reading the lane geometry ahead, assessing options.

The six were running too. And one of them was already on his phone.

— ◦ —

On a WhatsApp group called Referee Shesh Koro — Finish the Ref — the first message appeared at 10:49 PM.

*Bidhan Nagar Road-er **kache** spotted! Blue shirt grey pant. **Chhole** **asho**. — Spotted near Bidhan Nagar Road. Blue shirt, grey trousers. Come now.*

By 11:00 PM the group had 340 members. By 11:30, 1,200. By midnight, someone would have made a meme of Bikram's face with the caption: Chokh ache matha-te, kintu dekhe na. He has eyes in his head but does not see.

This was, Bikram would later think — in the single moment he had available for reflection during that night — extremely accurate.

— ◦ —

He lost the six in a lane off Ultadanga. Ducked through a gap between two buildings that a man of his dimensions should not have been able to fit through, moved along an interior passage that smelled of old plaster and cat, and emerged onto a parallel lane where a tea stall was closing and a dog was inspecting a pile of cardboard with professional thoroughness.

He stopped behind a parked truck and took stock.

Phone: thirty-one percent. Location: approximately two and a half kilometres from his guesthouse. Status: known. His face was on those replays and those replays were, at this moment, on every phone in a four-kilometre radius.

He opened Google Maps. The most direct route to his guesthouse passed through three major intersections and two areas that, based on the stadium's geography and the direction post-match crowds had dispersed, were almost certainly saturated.

He thought for a moment.

Then he turned north.

North Kolkata. The lanes.

This was either the best decision he would make tonight or the worst. He would not know which for some hours.

**Chapter Four**

# **The Cartography of Escape**

*11:12 PM – **12:05 ** **AM*

The Para Special Forces operational framework for evasion in complex urban terrain is not a set of instructions so much as a way of thinking. OODA: Observe, Orient, Decide, Act. The loop runs continuously — not as a conscious checklist but as a practised reflex, the way breath runs, the way a musician's fingers run, without the intervention of deliberation.

Observe: the lane system in North Kolkata is approximately the most complex urban terrain in Eastern India. Streets that are not streets. Buildings that share walls across gaps narrow enough to touch both sides simultaneously. Courtyards that open into other courtyards without announcement. The city here has not been built so much as accumulated — layer over layer across two centuries — and navigation by conventional means is a commitment to deep uncertainty.

Orient: Bikram had been in Kolkata for four months. He had not spent those months learning the gully topology of the northern neighbourhoods. He had spent them commuting between his guesthouse, the IFF liaison office in New Town, and the stadium, via the same three routes. His mental map of North Kolkata had the quality of a very rough draft.

Decide: move north and east, toward the density, toward the complexity. Counter-intuitive. In any conventional evasion doctrine, you move toward open terrain and legitimate egress points. But conventional doctrine assumes the pursuer is organised and geographically distributed. His pursuers were networked — but they were networked around the main arteries, the known routes, the places a man trying to get home would logically use.

The lanes were the one place a crowd cannot saturate, because saturation requires individuals who know where the lanes lead. Most of the people on that WhatsApp group were from this city, yes. But cities are not monoliths. A man from Behala does not know the gullies of Shyambazar the way a man from Shyambazar knows them, and a man from Shyambazar at midnight, after a football match and several hours of anger, is at home in bed.

Act: he went in.

— ◦ —

The first hour in the lanes taught him things.

He learned that the gullies of North Kolkata have a logic that reveals itself only in motion — that what appears from above as undifferentiated density is a system, with grain and direction, the way wood has grain. You cannot move perpendicular to the grain without slowing down and making noise. You can move with it at a surprising pace.

He learned that the city at this hour was not asleep. Small lights in upper windows. The low sound of television through walls — news channels, because everyone was watching the replays. He heard his own yellow card decision described on someone's television as he passed below a window, and the commentator's voice had the particular tone that broadcasting reserves for acts of historic incompetence — measured, sorrowful, inexorable.

He learned that the lanes had their own population. Night-shift workers on cycles. A woman carrying vegetables from somewhere to somewhere. An old man in a white dhoti sitting in a doorway with the untroubled air of someone who has seen everything this city has ever produced and is no longer capable of surprise. The old man looked at Bikram. Bikram nodded. The old man looked away.

He moved.

— ◦ —

The WhatsApp group was generating real-time coordinate updates. Someone had figured out the approximate vector of his movement — not through sophisticated tracking but through the very human method of aggregating eyewitness reports. Spotted near Shyambazar turning. Heading north, no longer on main road. Someone check the lanes off Mirzapur Street.

Bikram did not know this. His phone was dark, conserving battery. But the network was contracting around him regardless, the way weather contracts around a low-pressure system — not by plan, but by the accumulated logic of many independent decisions arriving at the same point.

He came out of a lane near the edge of Shyambazar and almost walked directly into an adda.

Eight men around a tea stall. Plastic chairs arranged with the geometric permanence of furniture that has been in exactly this arrangement for years. The conversation — he caught it as he slowed, pulling back into the shadow of a doorway — was entirely about Crescent United's 1997 form, specifically a match in November of that year in which a refereeing decision had also, apparently, cost them dearly. They were not discussing tonight's match. Tonight's match had excavated a communal memory and they were now inside it, forty thousand people's grief compressing itself into eight men and a tea stall at midnight.

He was five metres away. Street-light adjacent.

One of them looked up.

Bikram was already walking. Not running — running was signal. Walking. The unhurried, purposeful walk of a man who lives in this lane and is returning from somewhere entirely unremarkable.

He rounded the corner.

Behind him: Oi shey na? Oita ki referee? — Wait, was that him? Was that the referee?

He heard the subsequent discussion — three distinct opinions, no consensus, the 1997 match reasserting itself as the dominant conversational thread before anyone could verify their sighting.

He was already two lanes away.

**Chapter Five**

# **The Wrong Crowd**

*12:05 AM – 12:34 AM*

He heard the dhol before he turned the corner.

Riverside Athletic's supporters had organised, spontaneously, the way victory organises its witnesses: into celebration, into motion, into noise. A procession of approximately forty people was moving through a lane parallel to the one Bikram had been using, spilling at the junction where the two lanes met, firecrackers going off in small bright catastrophes overhead. The dhol was being played by a man who was either exceptionally talented or simply exceptionally enthusiastic — the music landing somewhere between the two, in that territory where rhythm lives when it doesn't need to be justified.

Bikram stopped at the junction.

The geometry of his situation: behind him, two men from the WhatsApp network who had, through reasonable deductive logic, identified his probable route. Ahead, this procession blocking the lane. To his left, a wall. To his right, the procession itself.

He did not hesitate long.

He turned right and walked into the procession.

— ◦ —

A green-and-white scarf appeared from somewhere — someone behind him, a Riverside supporter who assumed this unknown man was one of them, pressed it around his neck in the camaraderie of victory and slapped him on the shoulder and shouted something in his ear about the match. About the calls. About how finally, for once, the correct decision had been made. Bikram received the scarf and the enthusiasm with the expression of a man celebrating.

He was, for the next eight minutes, inside the procession. Moving with it. Green-and-white scarf. The dhol loud enough to mask most individual conversation. Around him, forty people who were elated by precisely the three decisions that had tonight made him the most hunted man in a five-kilometre radius.

The irony was not lost on him. He had the particular, bleak sense of humour that surfaces when circumstances become genuinely absurd — when the situation has moved past the point where normal categories of response apply.

He thought: I am wearing the evidence of my own catastrophe as a disguise.

He thought: the Army prepared me for many things.

He thought: not this specifically.

A man beside him, mid-forties, wearing a Riverside Athletic replica shirt with the number seven on the back, turned to him with the openness of victory and said: Bhai, bolo — kintu oi referee ta na thakle aaj hoto na. Tell me honestly, brother — without that referee, tonight doesn't happen. He meant it as praise. His face had the uncomplicated joy of someone whose team has just won and who has not interrogated the mechanism of the winning.

Bikram said nothing. He smiled in the way that someone smiles when the available alternatives are worse.

— ◦ —

The procession turned at a junction. Bikram did not turn with it. He slipped sideways out of the crowd at the moment the dhol swelled and everyone's attention went collectively left, ducked into a lane, removed the scarf, folded it, left it on a doorstep.

He checked his phone: twenty-four percent. The group had 890 members. Someone had posted a still image of him from the match broadcast — clearly in focus, the stadium lights catching his face in a way that left no ambiguity about identification. Someone else had written his full name. Bikram Dey. IFF referee. From Pune. Currently staying near Ultadanga.

Some of this was correct.

He put his phone away and kept moving north, and the city received him the way cities receive everyone moving through them at midnight — without ceremony and without comment, the indifference of a place that has seen everything and classified tonight as Tuesday.

**Chapter Six**

# **Elevation**

*12:34 AM – 1:08 AM*

The dead end appeared without warning, which is the only way dead ends appear.

Two rights and a left, moving fast now, reading the grain of the lane system the way he'd been reading it for the last two hours — and the lane simply stopped. A wall. Two-storey building on the left. Four-storey building on the right. Wall ahead. The lane behind him carried the sound of at least three people moving with the directed urgency of people who have been given a probable location and are closing on it.

Bikram looked up.

The four-storey building's drainage pipe ran from the roof gutter to a discharge point near the base of the wall. Iron. Old, but iron — the kind of iron that announces its uncertainty through sound rather than failure, giving you warning if you listen. The building's eastern face had window ledges at irregular intervals. The gap between the ledges and the pipe was approximately a metre and a half at the widest point.

His back had three healed vertebral fractures and an ongoing relationship with him characterised by frank communication about structural limits.

He grabbed the pipe.

— ◦ —

The first section held. He went up slowly — speed is how drainpipes come away from walls. Measured weight transfer, testing each section before trusting it. His arms were fine. His arms had always been fine. It was the rotation at the lumbar that the spine objected to, and climbing a drainpipe in the dark requires rotation in the specific way that a spine with three healed fractures finds unreasonable.

Second floor. A window ledge caught his foot and held it long enough to transfer weight off his arms. Third floor. The bracket at the fourth-floor level was, as he discovered with his left hand, not attached to the wall in any meaningful sense — it moved when he touched it, shifting in the masonry with the sound of something that has been pretending for years. He moved his weight off it and traversed left toward the window ledge in a movement that lasted three seconds and that he would not, if asked later, be able to fully account for.

He reached the roof.

He lay flat for a moment. The city spread in every direction. Flat rooftops, water tanks, a tangle of cable and washing line, the particular aerial geography of a city that has been improvising upward for two hundred years. To the south, the elevated light towers of the stadium still burning. To the east, the glow of Sealdah station. Below him, the lane: three men, phones out, flashlights scanning, looking at the dead end and then at each other and then down the lane with the frustrated geometry of people who know their quarry must be somewhere.

One of them said: Ghumiye parlo naki? — Has he gone to sleep?

Another said something Bikram couldn't quite hear.

The third: Mukesh-da chhilo, Sukanta Street dike giyeche bole. — Mukesh-da says he went toward Sukanta Street.

They went back the way they'd come.

— ◦ —

Bikram moved across the rooftops.

Three buildings, sharing party walls, forming a connected platform at approximately the same elevation. He moved at a crouch — not stealth exactly, but quietness, the habituated quietness of someone who has spent years in environments where noise carries consequences. The washing lines were the primary navigation hazard. He ducked two, displaced a third, which swung gently in the warm air behind him. Below, the city hummed its midnight frequencies.

There was something, he found, in the view from up here that the lane system didn't give you. Perspective. From down there, in the gullies, you were inside the problem — every decision immediate and local, every metre of distance bought at the cost of a decision. From up here, he could see the system. The shape of the neighbourhood. The way the lanes ran with the grain of an older city that the current one had grown over. Three-thirty in the morning in North Kolkata and the rooftops were a different world entirely — cooler, quieter, the smells of the city rising from below in waves rather than hitting you in the face.

He found a stairwell on the third building — half-constructed, a project of indeterminate vintage, open to the sky at the top, concrete steps that ended in rubble on the first landing and resumed on the second. He went down carefully. Reached the ground floor. A room full of darkness and plastic sheeting and the smell of ambition deferred. A gap in the wall where a door would eventually be installed. He went through it and out onto a lane he'd never been on.

He stood still. Listened. Clean.

He turned east, and kept moving.

**Chapter Seven**

# **Chotu**

*1:08 AM – 1:52 AM*

The cycle repair stall occupied a corner in a lane near Sovabazar with the permanence of something that has been in exactly this spot long enough to become part of the lane's identity. A narrow wooden platform. Tool hooks on the wall. A kerosene lamp burning low. And a fourteen-year-old boy who was not, technically, supposed to be up at this hour, but whose understanding of what was and wasn't his business differed substantially from the conventional.

Chotu — not his name, but what everyone called him, for reasons that had to do with his dimensions at age eight and had not been updated since — looked up from a punctured inner tube as Bikram came around the corner at a pace that was not quite running.

He assessed the situation with the speed of a boy who has grown up in a lane where situations can develop quickly and assessment is a survival skill.

He said, in Bengali: Ki hoyeche, referee babu?

What happened, referee sir?

Bikram stopped. Looked at him. The boy had the kind of face that dispenses with the preamble of polite distance — he just looked back, direct and curious, finding the situation interesting rather than alarming.

Tumi chino amare? — You know me?

Shobai chinee. Everyone knows you.

A pause.

Chhotkelna, said Bikram. It means roughly: don't be funny.

Ami funny hocchi na. I am not being funny. Chotu held up his phone. Bikram's face — match broadcast, stadium lights, unmistakable — was the group's cover image. Ek ghanta dhore tokhon eka eka haasche. Daroon. I've been laughing to myself for an hour. Brilliant.

Bikram looked at the boy for a long moment.

Tumi ki amake sahajjo korbe? — Will you help me?

— ◦ —

Chotu considered. This was not a moral deliberation so much as a commercial one. The boy had the pragmatic philosophy of someone whose life had not provided evidence that institutions of any kind — the thana, the school committee, the Bengal Football Association, the IFF — were particularly useful to people like him, and was accordingly predisposed to subvert them when it was entertaining to do so. The constable who had declined to file a report about his stolen cycle three years ago had made a lasting impression.

He said: Dui-shho taka.

Two hundred rupees.

Bikram paid.

— ◦ —

What followed was the most efficient intelligence briefing he received all night.

Chotu had a detailed and specific knowledge of the lane topology between Sovabazar and the Howrah Bridge approach — not out of geographical interest, but because he moved through those lanes several times a day on errands that required speed and the avoidance of certain adults. He explained, with the crisp economy of someone for whom local geography is operational rather than academic, which lanes were dead ends, which ones were through-lanes that merely appeared to be dead ends, where the clusters from the WhatsApp network had last been sighted. He got real-time updates from his cousin, who was — entertainingly — a peripheral member of the group and was monitoring it with the same detached amusement as Chotu himself.

He also had a bicycle.

Technically, the bicycle belonged to his neighbour. The neighbour was asleep. Three spokes were missing from the rear wheel and the chain needed oil and the saddle was calibrated for a person of substantially different dimensions than Bikram. But it was a bicycle, and the lane system was navigable by bicycle at a pace that materially changed the escape calculus.

Bikram took it.

Mairi bolo, said Chotu. Swear you'll return it.

Mairi, said Bikram.

One more thing.

He handed over a half-eaten packet of chanachur — spiced puffed rice, slightly stale. The hour was nearly two in the morning. Bikram had not eaten since six PM.

He ate the chanachur as he pedalled. The lane was dark and the three-spoked rear wheel produced a rhythmic irregularity that was meditative in its persistence, the particular percussion of imperfect motion.

Aar kabhu referee korben na, Chotu called after him. Never referee again, uncle.

Ditto, said Bikram.

He turned east and kept going and the kerosene lamp behind him receded and vanished around the corner's turn, and he was alone again with the city and the sound of the broken wheel and the night air that was beginning, very slightly, to carry the first suggestion of the river.

**Chapter Eight**

# **Tapas-da**

*1:52 AM – 2:28 AM*

He found the lane near the Kali temple by following the sound of the bells — distant, irregular, the bells that ring when someone presents an evening offering, a sound that has no relationship to any specific time of night and can appear without warning from deep in the city's interior, cutting through traffic and conversation with the calm authority of something that has been doing this for a very long time.

He didn't need the temple. He needed the doorstep beside it, because his back had begun its third extended communication of the evening about the structural limits of the lumbar vertebrae, and the bicycle had become unsustainable over the uneven lane surface.

He parked it against a wall and sat on a step.

The man on the adjacent step — he had not noticed him initially, the figure was that still — said, in Bengali: Chai khabe?

Do you want tea?

— ◦ —

Tapas Mukherjee was fifty-three years old. Retired secondary school teacher, history subject. Resident of this lane for forty-one years, in the same flat his parents had rented in 1972 and that he had eventually taken over and eventually stopped thinking of as rented. A man whose face had the specific quality of someone who has read a great deal and concluded that reality is consistently more complicated than its representations, and has made a kind of peace with this.

He had a flask. He poured tea into a small clay cup — the kind you get from stalls, unglazed, slightly porous, that holds heat longer than it has any logical right to — and handed it across without further preamble.

Bikram took it. He was not, at this moment, in a position to evaluate the social geometry of the offer.

They sat in silence for a moment. The city made its two AM sounds: a dog somewhere, a truck on a distant arterial road, the bells fading at their own pace.

Tapas-da said, without looking at him: Tumi ki jaano ki bhul korecho?

Do you know what you did wrong?

Bikram drank his tea. He thought about the question with the seriousness it warranted.

Haan, he said. Yes.

Tapas-da nodded. Looked at the lane. Ami chollish bochhor Crescent United dekhchi. I have been watching Crescent United for forty years.

Jani, said Bikram. I understand.

— ◦ —

A pause. The bells again, briefer.

Tumi ki Army te chhile? — Were you in the Army?

Bikram looked at him.

Haat er grip, said Tapas-da. Your grip on the cup. He mimed it — thumb placement, contact surface. Amar boro chele CRPF-e. My elder son is in the CRPF.

Haan. Para, said Bikram.

Para. Tapas-da absorbed this. Tarpore ki holo? What happened after?

Bikram told him. The training accident. The vertebrae. The desk they offered him. The referee's course. Colonel Ghosh. The shortage that everyone in Indian football administration deplores publicly and does nothing about privately because the solution requires investment and investment requires priority and this level of the game has neither. The Level-1 certification he'd passed barely. The optometrist in Ultadanga and the report folded into quarters in his jacket pocket.

Tapas-da listened. He did not offer commentary while Bikram spoke. He refilled the cup when it was empty. When Bikram finished, he was quiet for a moment.

Then: Amar school-e amar principal chhilen — retired Army officer. Bhalo manush. Pathshala chalabar kono dharna chhilo na. My school's principal was a retired Army officer. Good man. No idea how to run a school.

A pause.

Mara gechhe? — Is he dead?

Haah. 2019-e.

Bhalo administration korchhilen?

Na. Kintu try korchhilen. No. But he tried.

— ◦ —

Bikram held the small clay cup in both hands and thought about the distinction. Between trying and being able. The space between those two things — that was where tonight had happened. That space was where a lot of things happened, in a lot of fields, in a lot of positions occupied by people who were willing and present and trying, but not precisely equipped. The system put them there. The system had a vacancy. The vacancy needed a body. The body arrived. The vacancy was technically filled. And then, in the ninety-third minute of a Bengal Shield qualifier, forty thousand people paid the price.

Tumi ki football-ei referee korte cheyo chhile? — Did you actually want to be a football referee?

The question was direct enough to be surprising. Bikram considered lying. It was a reflex — the operational reflex of a man who has spent years in situations where the correct answer and the honest answer occupy different positions.

Na, he said.

No.

Tapas-da did not respond to this. He looked at the lane. After a long moment: Jao. Sondhe theke onek loker pathe ache, shesh raate ektu kome jabe. Go. The evening had many people on your trail. Late night, it thins out.

He said nothing else. He didn't call anyone. He didn't move from the step.

Bikram stood. His back spoke. He listened and went anyway.

Kaka, said Tapas-da, without turning.

Bikram stopped.

Bhool manush-ra-o match-e ashe, dekhe, haashe, kaaande. Etao kichu kum na. — Even wrong men come to the match, watch, laugh, cry. That's not nothing.

Bikram stood with this for a moment. Then he picked up the bicycle and went.

**Chapter Nine**

# **The ****River**

*2:28 AM – 3:44 AM*

The Hooghly at this hour was a different element than it was in daylight.

In the day, the river is colour — brown-green, shifting, the colour of old tea diluted by rain, a thing of specific visual weight. At three in the morning it was darkness with current, darkness that moved, darkness that had temperature and width and the low-frequency sound of water moving against stone it has been moving against for a very long time. The ghat steps ran down to the waterline in the way of Kolkata ghats: wide at the top, narrowing, ancient, made of stone that has absorbed everything the city has spent two hundred years discarding into it.

Bikram leaned the bicycle against a wall near the ghat and stood at the top of the steps and looked at the river and thought about the bridge.

The Swapna Setu — the Bridge of Dreams, the great suspension bridge, the one that appears on every postcard — was four hundred metres to his left. He could see its cables against the orange-pink of the city's reflected light, a geometry of tension and counter-tension that has always struck him as unusually honest about infrastructure. A bridge that shows you exactly what it's doing to stay up.

He needed to cross.

— ◦ —

His phone — twelve percent, rationing — had a message from Chotu's cousin, who had added Bikram to a secondary group of people monitoring the hunt for entertainment rather than participation. The message said: Howrah Bridge-er pedestrian lane-e ache koyekjon. Rokho. There are several people on the bridge's pedestrian walkway. Wait.

He didn't know how many several meant, or how organised. But he could extrapolate. The hunt had been running for five hours. The main group was enormous. The people still out at three-forty AM were not the ones who'd flagged — they were the core, the committed, the ones whose anger had lasted long enough to become something else: a kind of fidelity to the feeling. Eight, he estimated, based on the crowd physics of how collective anger sustains itself across a night.

He looked at the river.

He looked at the bridge.

He thought, briefly, about the third option.

There are no boats at this hour.

He looked at the bridge again.

— ◦ —

Observe: the Swapna Setu. Pedestrian walkway on the upstream side. Length approximately seven hundred metres. Width of walkway approximately three metres. Trucks heading into the city for the early morning wholesale markets using the main carriage lanes below — present, therefore noisy, therefore cover. The walkway itself would not be empty at this hour, but foot traffic would be sparse and purposeful.

Orient: his pursuers on the bridge were waiting. Static. Static positions on a long, narrow structure are strong if the target is predictable and weak if the target controls pace and timing. The target controls pace and timing.

Decide: the bridge.

Act.

He left the bicycle at the ghat with a silent and genuine apology to Chotu's neighbour, and stood for a moment looking at the river — the dark movement of it, a distant barge light moving upstream against the current — and then turned and walked toward the bridge approach.

The city was quiet in the way that a city is quiet only for an hour or so, the period between the last of the night and the first of the morning when even the dogs take a break. He walked through it and the footpath was empty and the shops were shuttered and the only light was the irregular yellow of sodium streetlamps and the constant ambient glow of the city against low cloud.

He reached the approach ramp.

He could hear them before he could see them.

**Chapter Ten**

# **Injury Time**

*3:44 AM – 4:31 AM*

The bridge approach on the Kolkata side has a specific sound architecture — the trucks coming off the main span create a sustained bass note that the concrete structure amplifies, and voices carry in unusual ways underneath the overhang. He heard two voices, then a third, positioned them forty metres ahead and to the left, near the pedestrian entry point.

He adjusted his route. The bridge approach has vehicle entry lanes and pedestrian access points and, underneath both, structural space — maintenance walkways, service access, the architectural underside of a major piece of infrastructure that needs periodic inspection and has provision for the people who perform it. Bikram had noted this on previous crossings, the way he noted structural details out of a habit that had been trained into him before it became natural.

He went underneath.

— ◦ —

The maintenance walkway was narrow and ran parallel to the vehicle carriage for approximately two hundred metres before rejoining the pedestrian level at a mid-span service door. The door was not locked. Infrastructure of this vintage and this city rarely has everything locked — there are too many things to lock and not enough institutional will to lock all of them, which is, Bikram thought, not unlike the IFF's referee programme.

He went through.

The bridge at its mid-span is a different environment than its approaches. The Hooghly is wide and the river wind comes off the water with sustained force, and the sound of the bridge itself — the vibration of the cables, the mechanical conversation of a structure under load from ten thousand vehicles a day — is surprisingly alive. Not loud. Alive. He could feel it in the soles of his feet. The bridge was working and you could feel the work.

The group he'd heard at the approach had split. Two had come up onto the pedestrian walkway. He registered them when he emerged from the service door — two men, his approximate age, moving along the walkway in his direction with phones out, flashlights scanning the dark ahead of them with the methodical sweep of people who have been doing this for hours and are operating on a diminishing reserve of certainty.

Three options: the walkway, compromised. The maintenance lane back, insufficient progress. The outer railing.

He looked at the outer railing.

— ◦ —

The railing of the Swapna Setu is not designed as a climbing aid. It is designed to prevent people from going over it, which is a different engineering intention than facilitating anyone going around it. There is, however, a narrow service ledge on the outer face — a maintenance platform for cable inspection, one metre wide, running the length of the span — accessible from the walkway via a service gate at the mid-span point.

The service gate was not locked.

He went through it.

— ◦ —

The ledge was damp. River air does that — everything on the outer face of a bridge over a large river is permanently damp, the surface mineralised by decades of spray. Bikram moved along it with his back to the railing and his face to the river, which was seventy metres below him and visible only as moving darkness and the occasional stippling of light from a barge somewhere upstream. The cables above him were taut and wet and singing in the wind at a frequency too low to be heard but not too low to be felt.

His back spoke to him clearly about the railing-gripping position, about the lateral stresses, about the specific conversation they'd been having all night about reasonable requests.

He moved anyway.

— ◦ —

The two on the walkway took four minutes to reach the service gate. By then Bikram was sixty metres further along the span, moving carefully, methodically, one hand always in contact with the railing. One of them saw the gate was open. There was a shouted exchange — the two of them, deciding something, the decision visible in their body language even from sixty metres in the dark. One followed.

What followed was not a fight.

Bikram had no interest in fighting — fighting has sound and consequence and it was nearly four in the morning on a narrow ledge above a major river. What it was, was a management exercise. He knew the ledge. The man following him did not know the ledge. He moved faster. He kept sixty metres of wet, unfamiliar surface between them. He reached the Howrah-side service gate, went through it, walked quickly down the ramp, and came out on the Howrah side.

Behind him, from the bridge, a voice: Bhai! Ek minit! — Brother! One minute!

The voice of someone who is no longer angry, or not primarily angry. Tired. Surprised, perhaps, by the last five minutes of his own decision-making. The specific tone of someone who has been on a wet ledge seventy metres above the Hooghly at four in the morning and is now reassessing the evening's commitments.

Bikram did not stop.

He walked off the bridge and into Howrah, and the city on this side was a different city — wider, lower, the streets broader, the smell of the station reaching him even from half a kilometre away, diesel and samosa oil and the metal smell of tracks. He kept walking. Behind him the bridge was silent. The cables sang in the wind at their subaudible frequency.

He was across.

**Chapter Eleven**

# **Full Time**

*4:31 AM – 5:52 AM*

A chai stall near Howrah station opens at four-fifteen. This is a matter of economic logic — the station never fully closes, and the first trains of the morning begin loading before five, and the people who travel those trains need tea in the same way that all humans in this part of the world need tea: urgently and without apology.

The stall was run by a man of indeterminate age who had the look of someone for whom the concept of surprise had been retired sometime in the early nineties and had not been reinstated since. He looked at Bikram — dishevelled, slightly damp from the bridge, moving with the specific quality of a person who has been awake for twenty hours and has spent those twenty hours being very busy — and said nothing. He put a glass on the counter and began to pour.

Bikram sat on the wooden bench outside the stall. His referee's bag was still with him. It had been with him through all of it — the drainpipe and the rooftops and the procession and the ledge above the river — because the habit of not abandoning equipment is older than any of tonight's events and had outlasted all of them.

He put the bag between his feet. He took his phone out. Eight percent.

— ◦ —

He opened the WhatsApp group.

The group's last message had been at 3:58 AM. Chhede dao. Shesh. Let it go. Enough.

Below it, someone had posted a GIF of a waving hand. Then someone had posted a voice note — thirty-two seconds, he did not listen to it. Then someone had posted, with the lateral logic of groups at four in the morning, a recipe for mutton biryani.

The group had moved on. Groups do. Collective anger has a half-life and collective exhaustion reliably shortens it. Forty thousand people's grief had compressed itself into a hunt through the lanes of North Kolkata and across the cables of a bridge, and then it had dispersed the way these things disperse — not by resolution, but by the accumulation of tiredness in individual bodies, each person making the private calculation that a bed exists and that what you're still doing is no longer proportionate to what you're still feeling.

The city had absorbed tonight, the way it absorbs everything, and would be slightly different for it in ways that were not yet visible.

— ◦ —

He drank his tea. The glass was small and hot and contained exactly the right amount of tea, which is more than seems adequate and less than seems sufficient.

Around him, Howrah was beginning its day. The first coolie with a luggage trolley, moving at the purposeful pace of a man with a train to meet. Two women in a hurry, bags on heads, the hurry of people for whom delay has real costs. The early wholesale vegetable vendors setting up near the station approach, their crates stacked with the particular geometry of produce at dawn — aubergines, gourds, bundles of greens whose names he didn't know.

Above the buildings, the sky was doing something. Not sunrise yet — something earlier than sunrise, a lightening of the darkness, the sky above the city moving from black to the deep blue that precedes colour. The blue of a world that is deciding to begin again.

He thought about Farrukh Tashkentov. The boy sitting on the pitch with his knees drawn up and his eyes on the grass. The bewilderment. The specific innocence of a nineteen-year-old who has been told, officially, on television, in front of forty thousand witnesses, that something real that happened to him did not happen. Who will carry tonight in his body — the yellow card, the match, the outcome — for the rest of a career that is just beginning and that will outlast this incident by decades, probably. Who deserved better from the man in the black shirt.

He thought about Tapas-da on the step.

Bhool manush-ra-o match-e ashe.

Even wrong men come to the match.

— ◦ —

His phone rang.

He knew, before he looked at the screen, who it would be.

Bikram. Heard what happened. Damn mess. Are you alright?

Yes sir.

Good. Listen — Santosh Trophy qualifier. Siliguri. We have a vacancy, the referee shortage is—

The sky above Howrah. The first colour — not gold yet, something earlier than gold, a suggestion of orange in the east where the cloud mass was breaking and the horizon was deciding. A crow landed on the railing near the tea stall, assessed Bikram with the particular assessment of a crow — thorough, brief, conclusive — and flew off.

I'll call you back, said Bikram.

He didn't hang up. He sat with the line open, the call timer running, Colonel Ghosh at the other end presumably waiting in an air-conditioned flat somewhere in the city with a cup of tea of his own. He looked at the sky. He looked at his IFF referee certification card, which he'd taken from his bag and was holding in his left hand — the laminated card with the bad photo from the processing day and the federation seal and his name and his certification level and the expiry date, which was seven months away.

Seven months.

He looked at the card for a long time.

The chai stall man refilled his glass without being asked. The sun was coming. The sky was making its decision.

He drank his tea.

Injury time: over.

Match result: inconclusive.

Vacancy: still open.

# **Epilogue: The System**

Two weeks after the night of the Bengal Shield qualifier, the Indian Football Federation released a routine circular to regional associations. The circular noted an ongoing shortage of qualified referees at the sub-elite level and invited applications from retired sportspersons and physical education graduates for an accelerated Level-1 certification programme. The application process required passing a written rules examination, a fitness test, and a mandatory medical clearance from a certified physician.

The medical clearance required disclosure of any conditions affecting vision, spatial perception, or physical fitness.

In the four months since the programme's last intake, sixteen applicants had disclosed and been disqualified on grounds of visual impairment.

Twelve posts remained vacant.
