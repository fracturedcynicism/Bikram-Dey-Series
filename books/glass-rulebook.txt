THE GLASS RULEBOOK

A Novella

Being the fourth night of Bikram Dey

FRACTURED CYNICISM

"The glass rulebook: a set of rules everyone can see through
and nobody is allowed to mention."

---

Fourteen months ago, Bikram Dey made three wrong calls at a football match and spent the night running through Kolkata.

Eight months ago, he made one correct call at a combat tournament and spent the night fighting through Kolkata.

Two months ago he was assigned to protect an election.

He succeeded. The system recorded the success.

The system also recorded something else.

Three weeks later, his physiotherapist told him he was ready.

He renewed his certification.

This story begins in Tangra.

It ends at a set of stone steps at dawn.

Between those two facts is this.

---

**CHAPTER ONE**

**The Underground**

*Tangra, Kolkata — 9:15 PM*

Tangra smells of tannery and rain and something that the tannery left behind when it closed and the rain cannot fully dissolve. It smells of the business of living done at close quarters across a very long time. Kolkata's Chinatown — what remains of it, the Chinese leather industry gone now, the restaurants staying because restaurants outlast everything — occupies the city's eastern margin with the stubborn permanence of a neighbourhood that has been written off repeatedly and not received the notification.

The Dongfang Club was not on any map. It occupied the basement of a building on a lane off Tangra Road that had, at street level, a functioning auto parts shop and, at first-floor level, the offices of a logistics company whose function Bikram had not examined closely enough to characterise. The basement had been, at various points in its history, a storage facility, a printing press, a mahjong room, and currently a combat sport event space with a twelve-by-twelve canvas mat, two hundred folding chairs, and a ventilation system that predated the current owners and had outlasted several sets of opinions about its adequacy.

Bikram had been here three times. The first two had been straightforward. Underground events in Kolkata occupied a specific legal grey, the kind that persists because the relevant authorities have reached a collective assessment that the available alternatives are worse, and the grey produces a functional ecosystem: fighters, corners, a small but consistent paying audience, and officials willing to work at the rate the ecosystem supports.

The rate was not good. He was not here for the rate.

He was here because the alternative was sitting in his guesthouse near Ultadanga reading the fourth optometrist's report for a fifth time, and he had read it enough times to know that reading it again would not change what it said, which was that his depth perception was recovering and that recovery was dependent on reduced physiological stress and regular therapeutic intervention and adequate rest.

He had been doing two of those three things.

The third required not being at events like this one, which meant being at his guesthouse, which meant the report.

He had made a decision about the report. The decision was: later.

— ◦ —

Two bouts before the main event. He was watching from the canvas's edge, hands behind his back, reading the room the way he always read rooms now — exits, columns, the weight distribution of the crowd in the folding chairs, who was watching the fight and who was watching the doors.

The shoulder was at sixty percent. The knee had its position. The ribs — healed, technically, from Book Two, from the cold room, from the thing he'd never had treated properly — now carried compound damage from the election night. Two months ago, at Booth 42, he had taken impact at the counting centre gates that had reactivated the old fracture and created new trauma. The rib injury was worse than it had been. The healing trajectory had been set back. Dr. Wangmo had been precise about this: the next stress would cost him more. He was aware of the cost and proceeding anyway.

The back said nothing. The back had been saying nothing since the physiotherapy ended, which was either genuine improvement or the back recalibrating its strategy, and he had decided not to investigate which.

The main event was a Muay Thai contest. Both fighters were good — real good, not curated-for-an-audience good. The promoter, a compact Hakka-Chinese man named Vincent Lo who had been running this room since 2017 and had never once in Bikram's experience given him any cause for concern, ran clean events. The informality of the venue did not extend to the sport.

Bikram watched the fighters.

He was also watching the crowd.

This was not strategy. It was reflex. Twelve years of operational deployment had made the peripheral register automatic — the part of his visual field that isn't where you're looking is still processing. It doesn't require instruction. It just does it. It files what it finds.

At the ninth minute of the second round, it filed something.

— ◦ —

The man was in the third row. Right side. Corner seat — the seat nearest the service stairs, the one that faces both the canvas and the room's eastern exit simultaneously. This was not a coincidence. You don't take that specific seat by coincidence. You take it because you've assessed the room and you want visibility in two directions.

Bikram had noticed the seat choice. Filed it. Continued watching the fight.

Then the man turned his head.

Forty-five degrees. Looking toward the eastern exit. Something at the exit had his attention. In the turn, the room's single overhead light — one industrial pendant, positioned above the canvas, creating a hard central pool and a graduated shadow toward the edges — caught his face at a specific angle for approximately one and a half seconds.

The face triggered something in Bikram's memory.

Not recognition exactly. Recognition is conscious. This was earlier than recognition — the pre-conscious filing that happens when the brain's pattern matching encounters something it has stored under a specific classification. The classification was: classified. The brain said: this is from a room you signed documents about.

He kept watching the fight. He did not look at the man again.

He needed seventeen seconds.

In seventeen seconds, the Para SF's operational memory — the specific faculty that is trained to retain faces, positions, operational details from briefings, the kind of retention that isn't general memory but targeted and durable — produced the file.

Commodore Ashok Mitra. Retired. Indian Navy. Last known position: Technical Advisory Committee, Ministry of Defence, 2019. Before that: Directorate of Naval Design. Before that: joint briefing, J&K, winter 2011. A classified room. A table with six people. Mitra had been there in an advisory capacity — naval intelligence assessment, cross-service. Bikram had been there because his unit had been tasked and needed the briefing.

He had signed documents about that room.

He had not thought about that room for eleven years.

The man in the third row was Commodore Ashok Mitra and Commodore Ashok Mitra had no visible reason to be in the basement of a building in Tangra watching an underground Muay Thai event on a Wednesday night.

And there was another face his pattern-matching was trying to file.

An operative from two months earlier. The warehouse near the Hooghly. Election night. The incident he had documented as systematic election interference and filed with the election commission as an official incident report. A man named Arjun Bhattacharyya, according to Parveen's research. The man whose face Bikram had memorized during the warehouse ambush, using the Para SF protocol: never forget, never mention unless necessary.

The operative from the warehouse was connected to someone. To an organization. To a structure of coordination that Bikram had documented in his election night incident report — the same report the election commission had filed away with a note that the official might have experienced "pattern-matching bias" based on his operational background.

His vigilance about election night had been documented as paranoia in the institutional record.

And now, two months later, that paranoia was looking at a face in a Tangra basement and recognizing the connection it had been searching for.

Bikram kept his hands behind his back. He kept watching the fight. His face did not change.

Inside: the OODA loop initialising. Clean and fast, the way it does when the operational context is genuine.

*Observe:* face confirmed. Position confirmed. Seat choice: deliberate. His attention at 09:17 went to the eastern exit — someone was expected from that direction.

*Orient:* Mitra is not here for the fight. He's here for a meeting. The eastern exit leads to a service corridor and beyond that to a delivery lane on the building's north side. Private. Away from the street.

*Decide:* finish the bout. Do not engage. Do not look at him again. Exit through the west stairs after the bout, go east via the external lane, establish observation of the delivery lane before Mitra arrives at it.

*Act.*

The bout ran its last two minutes. Bikram called it clean. He raised the winner's hand. He spoke to Vincent Lo for the standard thirty seconds of post-bout formality. He moved to the west stairs.

Behind him, in the third row, the corner seat was already empty.

He moved faster.

**CHAPTER TWO**

**The Lane**

*Tangra — 9:47 PM*

The delivery lane on the building's north side was fourteen metres long, terminating in a gate that opened onto a secondary road running parallel to Tangra Road. It smelled of old leather — the ghost of the tanneries, embedded in the brick. Overhead the city's ambient light dissolved the stars completely. Two bare bulbs on the building's north face created overlapping pools of yellow. The gate was rusted iron, tall enough to be secure, old enough to have a gap at the hinge side that a man with specific experience would note.

Bikram was in the gap at the hinge side.

He had been there for ninety seconds when Mitra came out of the service door. Then a second man — not Mitra — who had come from the east exit. The meeting Mitra had been waiting for.

The second man: late thirties. Bengali, Bikram assessed from the limited physical markers available in bad light. Well-dressed in a way that was specifically calibrated to read as unremarkable — good fabric, conservative cut, nothing that would register to a casual observer as either wealth or its absence. A man who had learned how not to be looked at twice.

They stood. They spoke. Not long — four minutes, maybe five. Bikram caught fragments. The lane was narrow and sound carried in it with the efficiency of old brick construction but the fragments weren't enough for context. He caught: the name of a company — Meridian Technical Solutions, repeated twice, once by each man. He caught a date — December, unspecified year. He caught the English phrase — odd in a Bengali conversation, which meant it was a term with a specific technical meaning that they hadn't bothered translating — acceptance testing.

Then the second man passed something. Small. Flat. An envelope, the size of a mobile phone, passed with the specific motion of someone who has done this before: no preamble, no ceremony, the transaction absorbed into the movement so that if you weren't watching for it you would not see the moment of transfer.

Bikram was watching for it.

He had it. He had the face. He had Meridian Technical Solutions. He had December. He had acceptance testing. He had the transaction.

He did not have enough. Not yet.

He had enough to know that he was going to need considerably more.

— ◦ —

Mitra went back through the service door. The second man went through the gate.

Bikram waited three minutes. Then he went through the gate.

The secondary road was empty at this hour except for a parked tempo and a tea stall closing. He walked to Tangra Road, found an auto, gave his guesthouse address, sat in the back with his shoulder against the auto's side and watched the city move past and ran through everything he had just acquired.

His knee, sitting in the confined back seat of an auto-rickshaw, registered its standard position on confined seating. He noted it. He did not negotiate.

He thought about the 2011 briefing. What he knew about Mitra from that room: naval intelligence background, attached to a cross-service assessment of a specific operational scenario that Bikram had been read into for twenty-two hours and then read out of. Mitra had been calm, technical, precise. A man who operated through institutional channels with confidence — the specific confidence of someone whose power comes from systems they trust to sustain them.

That was 2011. It was now 2025.

Fourteen years. Retired. And in a basement in Tangra on a Wednesday night, taking a hand-delivered envelope from a man who knew how not to be seen.

He had been here before. Not physically. In the territory of the mind. Two months ago, at the election counting centre, watching the EVM data come in clean, knowing that the interference had been prevented, understanding that the system would document his prevention while questioning whether the threat had been real. The election commission had filed an incident report. His incident report. And they had recommended: the official's actions were appropriate, but his threat perception may reflect pattern-matching bias.

Pattern-matching bias.

Now, two months later, his pattern-matching had filed a face. Mitra's face. Mitra from the 2011 briefing. Mitra connected to Meridian. Mitra in a delivery lane.

And the pattern was: what is Commodore Ashok Mitra doing in a basement in Tangra taking an envelope for acceptance testing from someone who knows how not to be seen?

The space between those two facts was a story.

Bikram did not know the story yet.

But the pattern-matching — the thing the election commission had documented as bias — was telling him he was going to find it.

He was going to find it because his credibility had been damaged by being right about the election night threat, and he needed to restore it, and the only way to restore it was to be right about something larger.

That thought, that understanding of why he was about to take this on, that knowledge of how his institutional vulnerability had been weaponized into his investigation — that was the moment Glass Rulebook truly began.

— ◦ —

The guesthouse. His room. He sat at the desk. His laptop was a 2019 ThinkPad that he had bought secondhand after the football night because the old one had been in the car the escort drove away and he had never retrieved it. He opened it.

The first search: Commodore Ashok Mitra, Indian Navy, retired.

The public record was thin but navigable. Navy website — a standard retirement announcement, 2020. A photograph that confirmed the face. Member of the Technical Advisory Committee, 2018-2020. Before that: Joint Director, Directorate of Naval Design, 2014-2018. Education: NDA, Naval Engineering, postgraduate work in UK.

Second search: Meridian Technical Solutions.

A company. Mumbai-registered. Incorporated 2018. Director: three names, none of which meant anything on their own. Services: technical consulting, project management, maritime engineering advisory. Clients: not listed. Revenue: not public.

Third search: Meridian Technical Solutions naval contract.

Nothing directly. A 2022 article in a defence procurement newsletter. A reference to a consortium — three companies — bidding for a naval turbine maintenance and upgrade contract. One of the three: Meridian Technical Solutions. The contract: naval propulsion system work, associated with the DRDO's ongoing turbine development programme.

He stopped.

He read the article again.

DRDO turbine development programme. A domestic project. Started 2015. Intended to produce an indigenously designed naval propulsion turbine, reducing dependency on imported systems. Budget: substantial. Timeline: first acceptance trials, 2021-22. Status of the 2022 article: trials outcome pending.

Fourth search: DRDO naval turbine trials outcome.

A 2023 article. Brief. A Ministry of Defence committee report on the trials. Outcome: trials inconclusive. Significant technical deficiencies identified. Recommendation: programme pause pending independent technical review.

Fifth search: independent technical review committee members.

Six names. One of them: Commodore Ashok Mitra, Retd.

Bikram sat back.

His ribs said something. The election night injury was still filing grievances. He registered it. Continued.

Sixth search: naval turbine import contract India 2024.

A ₹4,200 crore contract. Awarded 2024. Bidding consortium: three companies, one of which was a French marine engineering firm with significant presence in the Indian defence market. The contract was for imported naval propulsion systems to fill the gap created by the domestic programme's failure.

He sat with this for a long moment.

A domestic programme fails in its trials. The failure is certified by a committee. One of the committee members is also, two years later, associated with a company in the winning bidding consortium for the import contract that the domestic programme's failure made necessary.

He looked at the screen. The screen looked back.

He had the shape of it. Not the proof. The shape.

His knee had the same view as always. His shoulder was maintaining its position. The ribs were maintaining theirs, though with less grace than they used to have.

He closed the laptop. He lay down on the bed. He stared at the ceiling.

He thought: I am two months out of the election night assignment. The physiotherapist told me recovery depends on reduced physiological stress. The election commission has documented that my threat perception may reflect bias. Colonel Ghosh is watching me carefully. The AIFSFO has noted that I operated outside my remit when I took the election night assignment.

He thought: I saw a face. I followed it. I have the shape of something larger.

He thought: I should call Colonel Ghosh. But not yet. Not until I have more than a shape.

He looked at his phone. The time was twelve forty-seven AM.

He put the phone down. Not yet.

He closed his eyes.

He did not sleep for a long time.

When he did, he did not dream about the shape. He dreamed about the 2011 briefing room — cold, institutional, the specific fluorescent quality of a room that is used for classified things and has absorbed the gravity of them over years. Mitra across the table. Calm. Precise.

A man who trusted the systems he operated through.

And Bikram understood, in the dream, that the election night had been a test of those systems. That the Hastings Circle — if such a thing existed — had been assessing how far they could push before the system resisted.

And he, at Booth 42, had been the system's resistance.

And the system had documented his resistance as bias.

Bikram woke at four AM. He made tea. He opened the laptop.

He started finding the rest of it.

**CHAPTER THREE**

**The Hastings Circle**

*Kolkata — Two Days*

He gave himself forty-eight hours. This was not an arbitrary number. Forty-eight hours was the window before the absence from his officiating schedule would require explanation. He had one scheduled assignment in five days — a boxing qualifier in New Town. Five days was plenty. Forty-eight hours was what the situation warranted right now and he would reassess after forty-eight hours.

He spent most of those forty-eight hours at the desk.

The material existed. This was not a buried conspiracy. It was worse than a buried conspiracy — it was a visible one, operating in plain sight, through institutional channels so legitimate that the visibility was itself the protection. The glass rulebook: rules you can see through and cannot mention.

And the election interference at Booth 42 had been part of this. The test of how far you could push the system before the system pushed back. The assessment of which officials would break under pressure, which could be worked around, which would enforce protocol with actual authority.

Bikram's refusal at the checkpoint had answered that question: this official cannot be worked around through administrative procedures.

And so the Hastings Circle had moved to their larger operation. The one that required neither election interference nor EVM manipulation. The one that had already succeeded.

He built it piece by piece.

— ◦ —

The Hastings Circle — he gave it the name himself, working alone, needing something to call it — had five members.

Lieutenant General Pradeep Subramaniam, 67. Retired 2019. Former Chief of Integrated Defence Staff. A significant career. A significant reputation. His foundation — the Subramaniam Strategic Studies Initiative — published defence policy papers. His papers on indigenous procurement consistently identified systemic failures in domestic capability. His papers did not identify that the systemic failures were, in several documented cases, assessed by committees on which he or his associates had sat.

Commodore Ashok Mitra, 58. Naval engineering specialist. The link between the DRDO assessment committee and the Meridian consortium Bikram had already found.

Brigadier General Ranjit Hora, 63. Retired 2021. Former Director General of Quality Assurance — the body that certifies defence equipment for procurement. His tenure had overseen the certification process for three major import contracts. In two of those three, the domestic alternative had been assessed as deficient by a committee whose membership overlapped with Hora's professional network in ways that the relevant ministry's conflict of interest registers did not fully reflect.

Dr. Sundar Krishnamurthy, 61. Civilian. Former Joint Secretary, Ministry of Defence. Retired 2022. Currently: board member, two defence consultancy firms, one of which was a partner in the Meridian consortium. His ministry career had included oversight of the procurement pipeline at the precise moment the domestic turbine programme entered its assessment phase.

The fifth member: harder to pin. A pattern in the paper trail rather than a named individual. Money moving through a foundation — the Coastal Heritage Trust, Kochi-registered, ostensibly a maritime cultural organisation — to the children and associates of the other four. Offshore, routed cleanly, the kind of routing that is not money laundering but is also not entirely explicable by the foundation's stated activities. The fifth member was the financial architecture. Bikram could not name them yet.

— ◦ —

The mechanism:

The domestic turbine programme was not a failure. This was the part that took him the longest to establish and the part that mattered most.

The programme had completed its development phase on schedule. The engineering — independently documented in two academic papers, one from IIT Kharagpur's maritime engineering department and one from the Naval Science and Technological Laboratory in Visakhapatnam — was sound. The trials of 2021-22 had produced results that the NLST engineers described, in internal communication obtained through RTI applications filed by a defence procurement activist and published online — Bikram had found them in a 2023 blog post with approximately two hundred readers — as within expected parameters for first-generation trials. Minor issues. Addressable. Normal.

The Hastings Circle's assessment committee reported significant technical deficiencies.

The gap between those two assessments — between the engineers who built it saying it was addressable and the committee saying it was deficient — was the story. The gap was not a matter of interpretation. It was a matter of specific technical claims in the committee report that contradicted documented trial data. Claims about vibration tolerances that the trial data did not support. Claims about thermal performance that the NLST engineers had specifically addressed and resolved in a supplementary report that the committee had received and not cited.

The committee had not been incompetent. Incompetence produces random errors. These errors were directional. Every error moved the assessment in one direction. One single direction.

Toward failure.

Toward the import contract.

Toward ₹4,200 crore.

Toward the offshore routing and the foundation and the children in London.

And the election interference at Booth 42 had been their test run on security systems. A way to assess resistance before they finalized the larger operation. A way to identify which officials would enforce protocol and which could be worked around.

Bikram had enforced protocol.

And so he had become someone they needed to account for in their risk assessment.

— ◦ —

He sat back from the laptop on the second evening. His eyes were tired. The fourth report said reduced physiological stress. The fourth report was in his pocket. He had not looked at it since reading it the first time and was not looking at it now.

His shoulder had a clear view of the last forty-eight hours. His knee was filing ongoing commentary. The ribs — quiet for most of the research, which he'd been doing seated and relatively still — had located a position that worked and were maintaining it with the focused determination of a negotiator who has found an acceptable arrangement and intends to keep it.

He thought about what he had.

He had the shape and most of the substance. What he did not have was a document. Something physical. Something that a committee or a court or — more relevantly — a ministry-level review board could act on. The public material was suggestive. The RTI-obtained data was compelling. The overlap of names and dates and financial flows was, to anyone with investigative training, obvious.

It was not obvious to the people whose job it was to make it obvious. Those people — the relevant oversight bodies, the defence ministry's internal audit, the parliamentary committee with nominal oversight of procurement — were either captured by the Circle's institutional network, or moving at institutional pace, or both.

What would make it obvious was the meeting notes.

Subramaniam and the Circle met at the Hastings Club. He had found this through three separate paths: a brief mention in a 2023 profile of Subramaniam in a defence magazine — a sentence about his 'regular Thursday evenings at his club'; a background comment in a journalist's Twitter thread about defence procurement that mentioned the club's billiards room as a known informal gathering point for retired service figures; and an RTI response from the club itself — which, as a registered institution with a heritage status, was subject to RTI in specific limited ways — confirming Subramaniam as a member since 2018.

The Hastings Club. Established 1827. On Chowringhee. Bikram had walked past it. He had never been inside.

He was going to go inside.

Not yet. Not without more.

He picked up his phone. He called a number he had not called in two years.

It rang four times. Then: a voice. Clear, direct, the voice of someone who answers every call the same way regardless of hour.

Brigadier Meenakshi Nair said: Bikram. I haven't heard from you since the No Contest business.

He said: I know. I need to talk to you.

She said: Are you hurt.

He said: Not today.

She said: That's a qualified answer.

He said: I have something.

A pause. He heard her close a door. Her voice, when she came back, was at a different volume — lower. She was at home. It was eleven PM. She had gone somewhere private in her house.

He told her what he had. He was concise. She did not interrupt.

When he finished, she was quiet for seven seconds. He counted.

She said: Subramaniam.

He said: Yes.

She said: He was Chief of Integrated Defence Staff when I was posted to the ministry. I attended three meetings he chaired.

He said: And?

She said: He was the most institutionally effective man I have ever encountered in my career. He knew every mechanism. Every process. Every lever. Nothing he did was outside the system. That was how he did it.

Bikram said: That's what I'm finding.

She said: What do you need from me.

He said: I need to know if you can receive something. If something comes to you, can you move it.

She said: If what comes to me is what you're describing, I have one person. One. In the current establishment. Who would act on it.

He said: One is enough.

She said: Bikram. These are not the people you've dealt with before.

He said: I know.

She said: The football crowd was forty thousand people who were angry. The combat sports syndicate was organized crime. This is different. These people are not angry and they're not criminals in the sense that criminals know they're criminals. They believe they're managing the system. They believe the system works better with their management. That belief makes them considerably more dangerous than someone who knows what they're doing is wrong.

He said: I know.

She said: They already know you were at the Tangra lane. Which means they know you're looking. Which means they're going to be prepared for you.

He said: I understand.

She said: Do you need help.

He thought about this. He said: Not yet. I'll call when I do.

She said: I'll keep the line clear.

He said: Thank you.

She said: Don't thank me. Just be careful with that body. The election night did something to you. I read Parveen's articles.

He said: I'm managing it.

She said: Are you. Because managing damage and continuing to generate damage are different things, and I'm watching you do the second one while claiming the first.

He said: I'll be careful.

She said: Goodnight.

He said: Goodnight.

He put the phone down. He looked at the window. The Ultadanga street below was doing what Ultadanga streets do at eleven PM — living at reduced pace, the day's business contracting toward the night's smaller necessities.

He thought: the Hastings Club. Thursday evenings.

He thought: I am someone the election commission has documented as having elevated threat perception. I am someone the AIFSFO is watching carefully. I am someone whose credibility has been damaged by being right about something the system would rather not acknowledge.

He thought: And so I'm going to be right about something larger, and the damage will compound, and the system will document that damage in ways that make my judgment increasingly suspect.

He thought: This is the glass rulebook operating exactly as designed. Making sure that the person who insists on institutional integrity is the person whose reliability is questioned.

Today was Tuesday.

He had two days.

**CHAPTER FOUR**

**Garden Reach**

*Kolkata — Day Three*

He went to Garden Reach first.

This was not the plan. The plan was reconnaissance of the Hastings Club. But Garden Reach presented itself on the way — a detour he had not planned and that the OODA loop told him, once he was in it, was not a detour. Was necessary.

Garden Reach. The industrial waterfront. The old dockyards. A city within the city, built for a specific purpose — the Garden Reach Shipbuilders and Engineers Ltd., operational since 1884, serving the Indian Navy and the coast guard and the inland waterways and anyone who needed a vessel built with serious intent in the eastern region. The facility was still operational. Active. Relevant. The dockyards produced things.

Beside the active facility: the retired docks. Berths that had not held a vessel in years. Sheds of corrugated iron, oxidised to the colour of old blood. The specific geography of industrial decommissioning — not abandoned exactly, not demolished, just present in the way of things that are no longer needed and have not yet been replaced by whatever comes next.

He walked along the waterfront road. His knee had opinions about the uneven surface. He walked anyway.

He was here because of a name in the research: Dr. Rajan Ghosh, 54, former project director of the domestic turbine programme's Kolkata component. The NLST had its primary facility in Vizag, but the programme had a secondary design office at Garden Reach — the proximity to the shipbuilders, the existing infrastructure, the institutional knowledge base. Dr. Ghosh had run that office from 2017 to 2023, when the programme was paused.

He was now a private citizen. He lived in Behala. He had given one interview — one — after the programme was paused. A trade publication. He had been careful in the interview. Measured. He had said, in the specific language of a scientist who knows exactly what they're saying and exactly what they're not saying, that the programme's engineering was sound and that the assessment had identified issues that his team believed were addressable.

He had not said more.

He had also, in the two years since the interview, filed three RTI applications about the committee assessment process. Two had been rejected. One was pending.

Bikram found him at the old dock's perimeter fence. Dr. Ghosh came here sometimes — Bikram had established this from a single social media post, eighteen months old, a photograph of the waterfront with a brief caption about missing the mornings. He was here now, standing at the fence, looking at the water. The same posture as someone waiting, except he was not waiting for anything specific. He was looking at a river.

Bikram stood beside him. He did not introduce himself. He looked at the river.

After a minute, Dr. Ghosh said: You're not here by coincidence.

Bikram said: No.

Dr. Ghosh looked at him. A careful look. The look of a scientist doing what scientists do with new data — classifying, assessing, deciding on significance.

He said: Who are you.

Bikram said: A sports official.

A pause. Then, unexpectedly: A small laugh. Dr. Ghosh said: That's either the best cover story I've heard or you're actually a sports official.

Bikram said: Both.

He told Ghosh what he had. Not everything — not Mitra, not the specific names. The shape. The technical gap he'd found between the trial data and the committee report. He described it in the language of the RTI documents and the academic papers, the language of the programme's own engineers.

Ghosh listened. The careful look did not change. When Bikram finished, he turned back to the river.

He said: The committee report has seventeen technical claims. Eight of them have no evidentiary basis in the trial data. Three of them directly contradict documented trial results. One of them — Claim 11, thermal cycling performance at sustained load — is the inverse of what the data shows. Not an interpretation error. The inverse.

Bikram said: What would it take to make that visible to a committee that could act on it.

Dr. Ghosh said: The original trial data. Not the summary in the committee report. The raw data from the trial facility. It's stored in two places — NLST Vizag, which is a controlled facility, and the programme's Kolkata secondary archive, which is in a building in Howrah that technically belongs to the Ministry of Defence and has been sitting empty since the programme was paused.

Bikram said: Address.

Ghosh looked at him. He said: You're not going to do something official with this.

Bikram said: I'm going to get it to someone who will.

Ghosh looked at the river for a long time.

Then he said an address in Howrah. A building number and a block. He said: The archive is on the second floor. Room 2-C. The door hasn't had a working lock since 2022, which I know because I filed three requests for maintenance and they were all processed as resolved without anyone visiting the building. There's a standard MOD padlock on the main entrance. 4-digit code. The code is the year the Garden Reach Shipbuilders was founded. Nobody changed it.

Bikram said: 1884.

Ghosh said: Yes.

Bikram said: Thank you.

Ghosh said: Don't thank me. And don't come to me again.

He went back to looking at the river. He had the posture of a man who had just made a decision and was choosing not to examine it too closely.

Bikram left him there.

— ◦ —

The building in Howrah was exactly as described. The MOD padlock: standard issue, 4-digit. 1884. The padlock opened.

The second floor. Room 2-C. The door: a standard internal door with a broken mechanism, closed but not locked, opening with a push.

The archive: twelve filing cabinets, a shelf of binders, a workstation with a computer that had not been powered on in approximately two years based on the dust geometry on the monitor. The filing cabinets were labelled by date. The trial period: late 2021, early 2022.

He photographed. He was systematic and he was fast and his phone's camera was the same one he'd used in No Contest and he knew its limitations in low light and worked within them. The relevant files: six cabinets' worth, the raw trial data, the engineering logs, the NLST engineers' own assessment notes.

He was there for forty-one minutes.

He sent the photographs to a secure file share with Meenakshi Nair's access credentials, which she had given him in a second call that morning. The upload took eleven minutes on the building's ambient wifi, which was operational because the building was still technically a ministry facility and the ministry had not remembered to disconnect it.

When the upload completed, he looked at his phone. A read receipt from Nair, immediate. Then a message: Received. Good. Thursday.

He put the phone in his pocket.

Thursday. The Hastings Club. The Circle's weekly meeting.

Today was Wednesday. He had tonight and tomorrow.

His ribs were communicating about what filing cabinets and photography required. He had felt them during the election night, during the warehouse sequence, during the gate breach. They were still managing their damage from then. Still negotiating their position.

He stood. He breathed.

He had the data. Nair had the data. What he needed next was not data. Data was the substance. What he needed was the meeting notes — the Circle's own record of the decisions made. The paper trail inside the institution.

For that he needed the club.

He left the building. He put the padlock back. He walked to the main road and found an auto.

He said: Chowringhee.

It was time to look at the Hastings Club.

**EPILOGUE: THE GLASS RULEBOOK**

The Ministry of Defence's internal review was initiated eleven days after the night at the Hastings Club. The review was formally classified. Its existence was reported three weeks later in a defence procurement newsletter with approximately four hundred subscribers. Parveen Siddiqui, who had the newsletter's editor as a source, published a story that had twelve thousand readers on the day it went up and was subsequently carried by two national publications.

The parliamentary committee on defence procurement added the domestic turbine programme to its schedule of enquiries in the following session. The DRDO's supplementary trial report — the one the committee had received and not cited — was entered into the public record.

Brigadier General Ranjit Hora's membership of the Hastings Club was quietly revoked. He did not comment publicly.

Dr. Sundar Krishnamurthy resigned from the boards of both consultancy firms. He cited personal reasons.

Commodore Ashok Mitra's connection to Meridian Technical Solutions was documented in the ministry review and referred to the relevant authority. The referral was pending at the time this account was compiled.

Lieutenant General Pradeep Subramaniam released a statement through his foundation expressing full confidence in the institutional processes and noting that his record of four decades of service spoke for itself. His lawyers filed three procedural challenges in the first week. All three were still active.

Dr. Rajan Ghosh was contacted by the ministry review as a technical witness. He agreed to testify. He continued his morning walks along the Garden Reach waterfront.

The AIFSFO governing board convened a special session regarding the conduct of national integrity official Bikram Dey. The session produced a resolution that was, in its language, a document of institutional complexity: it noted that the official had operated outside his formal remit, that the events of the relevant evening raised questions about the appropriate boundaries of an officiating appointment, and that the AIFSFO took such matters seriously.

It also noted that no misconduct finding was made.

The certification was not revoked.

But the asterisk remained. In the official record, there was now a second note: the official had, on two separate occasions in the preceding two months, demonstrated patterns of heightened threat perception that, while sometimes vindicated, had produced no concrete evidence of systematic institutional corruption in the first instance (election night) and had, in the second instance (the Hastings Circle investigation), resulted in an unorthodox and possibly illegal entry into a private facility.

The note did not say he was wrong. It simply said that his judgment required monitoring.

This was the specific institutional violence that would prepare the ground for Book 5.

Bikram Dey attended the boxing qualifier in New Town on Tuesday. He was not late.

He called four bouts. All four were clean.

Between the third and fourth bout he stood at the canvas edge and looked at the room — the lights, the crowd, the fighters in their corners, the specific charged atmosphere of a sport in the moment before it continues — and he thought about the glass rulebook.

He thought about it still being there. About its visibility and its persistence and the specific kind of faith it required — the faith that the visible can be safely ignored, that the rules can be seen through without being examined, that the system will sustain those who manage it.

He thought: it sustains them until it doesn't.

He thought: the call still stands when the room disagrees. That's what the call means. That's the only thing it has ever meant.

He stepped off the canvas.

He went home.

In the weeks that followed, as the ministry review moved through its processes and as the parliamentary committee scheduled its hearings and as Parveen published her investigative pieces with the methodical precision of a journalist who has finally found the shape she had been looking for, Bikram understood that the glass rulebook had done something to him that damage to his body had not yet done.

It had made him question whether the call was still the right thing to make.

Not because he thought he was wrong. But because being right was no longer sufficient. Being right now produced asterisks. Being right now produced scrutiny. Being right now produced the specific institutional doubt that said: your judgment requires monitoring.

And that doubt was more dangerous than any physical injury could ever be, because it operated not on his body but on his willingness to put the body at risk again.

It was the slow kind of institutional violence. The kind that doesn't leave marks.

It was the kind that made a man very carefully consider whether a third book was worth the cost of the second and first.

It was, in short, what the system always did to people who insisted on integrity.

It did not destroy them.

It made them question whether being destroyed would be worse than continuing.
