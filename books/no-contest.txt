**NO CONTEST**

*A Novella*

*Being the second night of Bikram Dey*

**Fractured Cynicism**

*"No contest: a ruling issued when a fight is stopped*

*due to an outside interference — neither fighter declared winner or loser.*

*The result is expunged. The night never happened."*

*Six months earlier, a man named Bikram Dey spent a night being chased*

*through the lanes of Kolkata by forty thousand furious football supporters.*

*He had made three wrong refereeing decisions. He survived.*

*He renewed his officiating licence seven months later.*

*He is still not entirely sure why.*

This story takes place in a different night.

The tournament final ends at nine-forty PM.

The flower market opens at two in the morning.

What happens between those two facts is this.

**Chapter One**

**The Call**

*9:22 PM – 9:41 PM*

The thing about officiating combat sport is that you watch the hands first.

Football had trained Bikram Dey to read bodies in space — trajectories, positions, the geometry of twenty-two men and one ball distributed across a hundred metres of grass. Clean geometry. Room to think. The mistake in Injury Time — as the sports press had taken to calling that particular evening, a phrase Bikram found both accurate and disproportionate — had been a failure of spatial perception at distance. His eyes. The nerve cluster. The report he'd kept folded in his pocket until it disintegrated.

Combat sport was different. You were close. Two metres from the fighters at all times. The geometry compressed to a square canvas no larger than a room, and the bodies in it were doing everything they could to reduce each other to unconsciousness through the most efficient application of force available. Bikram had found, to his own mild surprise, that he was considerably better at this than he'd been at football.

The nerve cluster was still degraded. The depth perception was still compromised. But at two metres, you didn't need depth perception the way you needed it across a pitch. At two metres you needed presence — the ability to read intent before it became action, to position your body in the negative space the fighters weren't using, to be everywhere and nowhere simultaneously.

This was, almost exactly, what twelve years in the Para Special Forces had been for.

He'd been thinking about this on the drive to the Sunrise Sports Complex, six months after the football night, with a renewed AIFSFO — All India Fight Sports Federation Officials — level-two certification in his wallet and no very clear explanation for why he'd gone and got it.

— ◦ —

The Kolkata Combat Championship final was the biggest event the AIFSFO's eastern region had hosted in four years. Two thousand spectators in a purpose-converted arena in Salt Lake. Corporate sponsors. A live streaming arrangement that put the broadcast in seven states. An AIFSFO chief official at ringside in a blazer and a dissatisfied expression. And at centre mat, with the fighters and their corners and the weight of several hundred thousand rupees in informal wagering, Bikram Dey in his referee's whites, hands behind his back, watching.

The final: Rajan Mitra versus Viktor Kasparov.

Rajan was twenty-six, Kolkata-born, Kalaripayattu base cross-trained into a hybrid system that was technically MMA but felt in practice like something older and more dangerous. He fought with the economical, unhurried precision of someone who has understood since childhood that force multiplied by accuracy beats force multiplied by mass every time. He was the crowd favourite. The crowd had been building his name into a frequency that Bikram could feel in his chest cavity.

Viktor was thirty-one, Georgian, built like something that had been designed for a specific industrial purpose. He fought on a wrestling-and-Sambo base with a ground game that had ended twelve of his last sixteen professional contests in the first two rounds. His corner spoke to him in Georgian and Russian and occasionally in a third language that Bikram couldn't identify and didn't find reassuring.

Rounds one and two had been clean. Good clean fighting. Rajan was faster. Viktor was more powerful. The contest was genuine — which is not always a given in tournaments of this structure, at this price point, backed by this particular constellation of regional money.

The third round started at nine thirty-four PM.

— ◦ —

Bikram was positioned at the north edge of the canvas when Viktor's corner worked on him between rounds. The corner man — compact, watchful, a man with very specific hands — administered something between the jacket and Viktor's ribcage in the twenty-eighth second of the rest period. A motion so small and so practised that it would have registered as nothing to anyone watching the fighters' faces, the score table, the crowd.

Bikram was not watching any of those things.

He was watching hands.

He saw it.

He knew what it was. Twelve years in environments where the administration of substances in non-sanctioned ways was a tactical consideration had made the recognition automatic. Not a definitive diagnosis — he had no kit, no laboratory, no certainty. What he had was a clear view, trained pattern recognition, and a rulebook that was unambiguous on the subject: a corner man administering any substance, stimulant, anaesthetic or otherwise, to a fighter during a rest period was a foul. The foul carried a mandatory ruling.

The bell rang for round three.

Viktor came to his feet with a specific quality of energy that Bikram noted and filed. The energy of a man whose threshold has just been chemically adjusted.

Twenty-two seconds into the round, Viktor drove Rajan to the mat and began the ground-and-pound combination that had ended eleven of those twelve professional contests. Rajan was defending. Barely.

Bikram moved to the edge of the exchange.

He thought: I am about to make a call with two thousand people in the room, seven states on the stream, and a man whose employers have a significant financial interest in this outcome who is sitting in the third row wearing a cream-coloured kurta and the expression of someone accustomed to outcomes going the way he has arranged them.

He thought: it is possible this will not go well.

He thought: a Para SF officer does not make a different decision because it might not go well.

He stepped in. One hand went up. He stopped the fight.

The signal for a corner foul is a specific gesture — both arms crossing at the wrist, then extending outward — that Bikram executed with the kind of clarity he'd once used for hand signals in operational terrain. Nobody in the arena could have been uncertain about what he was calling.

The silence lasted four seconds.

Then the man in the cream kurta in the third row — Sandip Chakraborty, forty-seven, second-generation Kolkata syndicate, known to the regional crime press as the Landlord for reasons having to do with the amount of property he'd acquired through means the property registry didn't fully reflect — took out his phone and made a call.

The call lasted eleven seconds.

Bikram, in the centre of the canvas with the arena erupting around him, did not know about the phone call. He would understand its content later, and thoroughly, in the way that understanding often arrives in his particular profession: through its physical consequences.

He disqualified Viktor Kasparov's corner. The fight was awarded to Rajan Mitra by corner foul. Rajan Mitra was the Kolkata Combat Championship.

Rajan, sitting on the mat where he'd been defending, looked up at Bikram with the specific expression of a man who doesn't yet understand what just happened but can tell, from the arena's noise, that it was significant.

Bikram helped him to his feet.

Viktor's corner said nothing. Viktor himself said nothing. They watched Bikram with the particular stillness that follows the moment when a situation has moved from one category to another and everyone in the room has registered the transition.

*Dead stop, thought Bikram. That's what this is.*

He didn't know, quite yet, how precisely accurate that was going to be.

**Chapter Two**

**The Cage**

*9:41 PM – 10:08 PM*

The post-fight protocol is the same everywhere: raise the winner's hand, coordinate with the judges for the official scorecard, ensure both fighters have medical assessment, prepare the post-event report. It is administrative, predictable, and normally takes about twenty-five minutes.

Bikram was three minutes into it when a man he didn't recognise entered the canvas from the east access point — the restricted access point, the one that required a specific lanyard — and walked toward him with the directional intent of someone who has a message to deliver and doesn't particularly care about the protocol it interrupts.

He was followed by two others.

Bikram's back-brain — the part that twelve years of operational deployment had turned into something permanently, involuntarily active, the way a smoke detector doesn't have an off switch — fired before his conscious mind had fully processed the entry.

He noted: three men, convergent vectors, the lead man's right hand was in his jacket pocket and his jacket pocket had something in it that changed the line of the fabric in a way that hands alone don't change jacket fabric. The other two were flanking. This was a formation. Formations mean coordination. Coordination means intent.

He stepped sideways off the canvas and put the ringside table between himself and the lead man.

The lead man said, in Bengali: Babu, cholo — which means roughly: come on, friend. The word for friend used in the way a person uses it when friendship is not what they mean.

Bikram said: I'm in the middle of post-event protocol.

The man looked at him. He was approximately Bikram's age, heavier, with the specific facial architecture of someone who has absorbed a significant quantity of professional violence over the course of his career and emerged from it with function intact if not aesthetics. He was not, in other words, inexperienced.

The AIFSFO chief official — the man in the blazer, whose dissatisfied expression had graduated, in the last three minutes, to something approaching horror — said from the far side of the ring: Bikram-da, is there a problem?

Bikram said, without taking his eyes off the three men: Yes.

— ◦ —

What happened in the next ninety seconds was not clean. Nothing real is clean. What happened was:

The lead man reached across the table. Bikram moved the table into the lead man using the leverage of both hands and his full body weight, which — despite the vertebral situation, despite the four months of physiotherapy, despite the continuing frank conversation with the lower lumbar — was still considerable. The table caught the man at hip level and drove him backward into the ring apron.

The second man came from Bikram's left. Bikram was already inside his reach — too close for the punch to generate the force it was designed for. The punch landed on Bikram's shoulder instead of his jaw. It was still a significant punch. His shoulder registered it. He registered its register and kept moving.

His elbow went into the second man's face with the compact, economical motion that Para SF close-quarters training produces — not a swing, never a swing, swings miss — a short driving extension that put the point of the elbow into the bridge of the nose. The second man sat down.

The third man had a weapon. Extendable baton, fully extended. He brought it down in a wide arc that Bikram slipped by dropping his right shoulder and letting the baton pass close enough to clip his ear. Then he was inside the arc, and the third man's wrist was in both of Bikram's hands, and the baton was on the floor, and the third man was face-down on the canvas with his arm in a position that made the joint's mechanics clear to both of them.

Bikram held the position. He was breathing. His ear was bleeding from the clip. His shoulder was going to be communicating its opinion for the next several days.

The lead man had recovered from the table. He was on his feet. He looked at his two companions — one bleeding on the floor, one face-down — and then at Bikram, and then he made the decision that the situation warranted and left through the east access point.

The entire arena had gone completely, profoundly silent.

The AIFSFO chief official was on his phone.

Rajan Mitra, new champion, still in his fight gear, was standing at the edge of the canvas looking at Bikram with the expression of someone who is revising, comprehensively, their understanding of what a sports official is.

*He said: Who are you?*

*Bikram said: I'm the referee. Help me move these two somewhere secure.*

— ◦ —

It took eight minutes to establish the following: the east access lanyard had been issued under a falsified credential to all three men. The arena's security staff — four people, two of whom had spent the last eight minutes hiding in the equipment room under the mistaken impression that a major riot was underway — had not issued the lanyards. The lanyards had come from the tournament's administrative team, one member of which had, as of approximately nine-fifty PM, stopped answering their phone.

Bikram listened to all of this while pressing a piece of ice wrapped in a corner towel against his ear and making his own assessment.

The assessment: the three men who'd entered the arena were local. Not professionals — the formation had been right but the execution had been soft, the decision to send three men with one weapon against an unknown quantity was an error that professionals don't make. These were the first response. The employer's first response to a problem is never their best response. The best response was being organized somewhere else, right now, while the first response had provided information about what they were dealing with.

Information they had now provided.

He looked at Rajan. Rajan was twenty-six, a champion, and at this particular moment, the man the syndicate had planned to lose this fight. The syndicate's planning had not included what to do if their fighter was disqualified and the underdog won. But Rajan had seen the man in the cream kurta make his phone call. Rajan had grown up in Kolkata. He understood, without being told, the implications of being on the wrong side of that phone call's outcome.

Bikram said: Do you have someone to stay with tonight? Not your home.

Rajan looked at him.

Bikram said: Answer the question.

Rajan said: My uncle. In Shibpur.

Bikram said: Go there now. Don't take a cab from outside the venue.

Rajan said: What about you?

Bikram considered this. His car was in the venue's basement lot. The venue's basement lot was the first place a professional would cover. His phone had the guesthouse number and nothing else useful. He had his wallet, his ID, his officiating credentials, and the ice-and-towel situation on his ear.

He said: I'll figure something out.

This was, in retrospect, an understatement.

**Chapter Three**

**The Kitchen**

*10:08 PM – 10:52 PM*

The Sunrise Sports Complex had been built in 1994 and expanded in 2011 and was, structurally, a building that had been designed by two separate architectural philosophies that had never been fully reconciled. The original building was practical and small. The expansion was ambitious and slightly incoherent. The result was a facility with twice as many corridors as it needed, three staircases of uncertain purpose, and an industrial kitchen in the east wing that served a banquet hall that had been closed since 2019 and had not yet been converted to anything else.

Bikram knew this because he'd spent forty minutes that afternoon doing what he always did before officiating at an unfamiliar venue: walking the space, reading the floor plan tacked behind the service entrance, noting exits, loading bays, the position of the generator room, the location of the service stairs. Old habit. The kind of habit you keep when the alternative — not keeping it — has consequences you've seen.

He took the service stairs down.

The banquet kitchen was unlocked. The equipment was still in place — four commercial ranges, two large ovens, stainless steel prep tables, a walk-in cold room at the far end, and a loading bay exit that opened onto the service road on the building's north side. The loading bay was not the car park. The loading bay was where the syndicate's people were not.

Yet.

— ◦ —

He was crossing the kitchen when he heard the service stairs door behind him.

Two people. The footstep pattern was specific: one heavy, one lighter, moving in the coordinated way of a two-person clearance sweep. Not the arena men. These moved differently. They moved like people who do this and are not, in any particular way, surprised to be doing it.

Bikram went left, behind the range bank. The kitchen was dark — he hadn't found the lights and was not going to find them now. The ambient light came from a strip of windows near the ceiling on the north wall, the exterior security lighting of the service road casting a pale yellow into the room. Enough to move by. Not enough to clearly identify faces at range.

He reached under the nearest range and found what ranges in professional kitchens accumulate: a flat-headed pot, a six-inch ladle, and approximately eight years of grease. He left the grease. He took the ladle.

This was not, he would be the first to admit, an ideal weapon. It was, however, what he had, and the Para SF's approach to available resources is empirical rather than sentimental.

The heavier footstep went right. The lighter went left, which was toward Bikram's position.

He waited. The thing about waiting — the thing that six months of football refereeing and two months of combat officiating had reminded him of — is that it requires you to be more still than the other person, for longer. Most people cannot do this. Most people's nervous systems generate motion. Shifting weight, adjusting position, the micro-movements of a body that is anxious and trying to manage the anxiety. Bikram's body did not do this. It had been trained out of it a long time ago in a curriculum that the Army described as psychological conditioning and that was, in practice, learning to be completely still for longer than your situation required.

The lighter footstep came around the end of the range bank.

Bikram hit the figure across the wrist with the ladle before they'd fully committed their weight. The strike was chosen specifically: not the head, not anything that would cause uncontrolled unconsciousness, but the complex of bones and tendons at the wrist that, if struck correctly with sufficient force, produces involuntary release of whatever the hand is holding.

The hand was holding a phone. The phone skittered across the tiled floor.

The figure — a young man, mid-twenties, not a fighter, a support person, an errand runner elevated above his grade — said something sharp and Bikram put him down with an arm lock that had his face against the prep table and his arm at an angle that the joint would accept without injury only if he remained perfectly still.

He said, quietly: Stay there. Don't move. I'm going to find your colleague.

The young man stayed there. He did not move.

— ◦ —

The heavier figure had heard. He was at the far end of the kitchen, near the cold room, and he'd heard the sounds well enough to tell him that his partner had not prevailed and that the asset they were here to acquire — or eliminate, depending on instruction — was still mobile and was now, measurably, dangerous.

He made the decision that the situation warranted, which was to go through the cold room.

The cold room was a walk-in refrigerated storage unit, six metres by four, running at approximately four degrees Celsius and containing nothing except empty wire shelving and the specific smell of cold rooms that have stored food for years and no longer store anything: a ghost-smell, the memory of provision. The heavy door was on a spring-close mechanism. Once you were inside, the only exit was the door you'd entered through or, at the far end, a delivery panel that would be locked from the outside.

Bikram followed him in.

This was, on the available evidence, a strategically questionable decision. Following an unknown opponent into a sealed cold room in a closed kitchen in a building currently being swept by hostile parties, with a ladle, was not what any rational tactician would recommend.

The tactician's consideration and the Para SF officer's consideration are, occasionally, different.

In the dark, in the cold, the disadvantage of superior size is considerably reduced. You can't use reach you can't see. You can't use a weapon effectively that requires you to know where the target is. What you can use is sound — breathing, footstep, the slight compression of air that a body moving in a confined space creates — and training to interpret that sound faster than the other person.

It lasted forty seconds. It was not pleasant for either party. It ended with the heavy figure on the cold room floor, breathing, a choice Bikram made consciously and specifically — he was a referee, not an executioner — with his hands around a shelving rack and the cold room's four degrees doing what cold does, which is make everything feel worse than it already was.

He left the cold room. He closed the door. He looked for something to secure it with. Found a floor mop, jammed the handle through the door's external bar.

He walked to the loading bay. Pressed the exterior release. The door rolled up on its tracks with the thunderous complaint of machinery that hasn't been serviced since 2019.

The service road was empty.

For now.

His ear was still bleeding. His shoulder was communicating at volume. His knuckles, from the cold room, had the specific tender quality of knuckles that have been used for a purpose their owner has not used them for in some time.

He walked out into the night. The Kolkata air hit him — warm, diesel-thick, familiar in the way of things that have become involuntarily familiar.

He thought: second verse, same as the first.

It was not, however, the same. Not quite.

**Chapter Four**

**The Journalist**

*10:52 PM – 11:29 PM*

Parveen Siddiqui was thirty-one years old, a sports journalist for a digital platform that covered combat sports in the eastern region, and had been accredited for tonight's final as press. She had press credentials, a camera, and the professional instinct of someone who'd been doing this job for six years: she was at the loading bay because she'd seen, from the press box, the disruption in the arena, and she had followed the most interesting person in the room.

She was standing behind a parked supply van when Bikram came out of the loading bay with blood on his ear and the expression of a man conducting an internal cost-benefit analysis.

She said: I've got the foul on video.

Bikram looked at her.

She said: The administration. Round three. I had a camera on the corner the whole time. I caught the whole thing. I was specifically covering Viktor's corner because I'd been tipped that the fight had alignment issues.

Bikram said: Tipped by whom.

By someone who is no longer reachable on their phone, since approximately nine-forty-three PM, she said. Which is when I started to understand that what I have on this camera is not just a story.

She held up the camera. A professional rig — mirrorless, solid glass — the kind of camera that produces footage that courts and licensing bodies and sports federations accept as evidence.

Bikram looked at the camera. Looked at her. Made the assessment that the Para SF makes and that is not the assessment most people in most situations make, because the assessment most people make is who is this person and can I trust them and the assessment Para SF makes is what does this person want, what do they have, and what are the consequences of various courses of action involving them.

What she wanted: to survive tonight with the footage, and presumably to publish it eventually, but survival first.

What she had: evidence of a sanctioned fight being chemically interfered with, which was worth something to the AIFSFO, something to the regional sports press, and considerably less to Sandip Chakraborty than it was to everyone else.

What the consequences were: that the footage made her a target and her being a target made her his problem. Not by any formal logic — he had no obligation to this woman. But the logic of the situation had its own momentum. She was here because she'd followed him. The people coming for him were, now, also coming for her.

He said: Do you have it backed up.

She said: Cloud synced twenty minutes ago. But they can compel the platform to take it down. The camera is the primary.

Keep it on you, he said. Don't let it out of your hands. Can you run?

She looked at him with the expression of someone who was expecting a different answer.

She said: Yes.

He said: Then we're moving.

— ◦ —

Her name was Parveen, she'd been doing this for six years, she'd covered three championship events that had later been found to have match-fixing elements, and her platform's editor-in-chief was, as she explained while they moved along the north service road at a pace that she was technically keeping up with, going to be furious in the way that editors are furious when their reporters acquire footage that will make their platform significant but acquire it in circumstances that compromise the legal situation.

She talked when she was stressed. Bikram noted this. It was not ideal for their current requirements, but she was managing her fear in the only way that worked for her and this was neither the time nor the context to address it.

He said: Who knew you'd be here tonight.

She said: My editor, my camera crew — I'm working solo tonight, which is unusual — the accreditation desk, and whoever gave me the tip.

He said: The tip came from.

She said: A message to my professional number. Anonymous. I traced the number, it came back to a SIM registered to a hardware shop in Shyambazar that closed in 2021. Someone who knew what they were doing.

Bikram filed this. Someone inside the syndicate's operation had wanted tonight documented. Someone who'd known the fix was in and had wanted, for reasons of their own, a record of it. The record existed. The record was now being carried by a sports journalist through a service road in Salt Lake at eleven PM, which was why they were moving and why stopping was not currently advisable.

He said: What's at the end of this road.

She said: The perimeter wall. Then the main road. Then the metro station, which is closed.

He said: What's past the metro station.

She said: VIP Road. You can get a cab.

He said: I can't use a cab. They'll have the registration system monitored.

She said: Are you serious?

He said: In my experience, people who can field three men in an arena and four in a kitchen within twenty minutes of a setback are organized enough to monitor vehicle registration systems. Yes.

A silence.

She said: There's a bus stop on VIP Road. The 44C runs until midnight.

He said: That works.

She said: It goes to Howrah.

He said, with the particular dry quality of a man who has had prior dealings with Howrah in adversarial circumstances: Of course it does.

**Chapter Five**

**The Georgian Problem**

*11:29 PM – 12:41 AM*

The 44C bus to Howrah was, at eleven forty-seven PM, carrying six passengers: an elderly man with a shopping bag, a young couple who appeared to be conducting an argument by not speaking to each other, a student with headphones, Bikram Dey, and Parveen Siddiqui. The driver had the thousand-yard stare of a man who has been driving this route since before most of these passengers were born and will continue driving it after they are all, in various ways, finished.

They'd been on the bus for eleven minutes when Bikram noticed the car.

White Innova. Three vehicles behind. It had made the same three turns the bus had made, which is not impossible — Salt Lake to Howrah has limited options — but the spacing was consistent. Not closing, not dropping back. Consistent. A following distance maintained with the precision of someone who has done surveillance before.

He said quietly to Parveen: Don't look behind us.

She said, at a volume appropriate to a bus: Why.

He said: Please just don't.

She didn't look. She was learning.

— ◦ —

The second piece of information arrived as the bus crossed the Ultadanga junction. Parveen's phone — she had it out, she was monitoring the AIFSFO's official communications channels and her platform's breaking news thread — showed a photograph. Surveillance quality, slightly grainy, taken from a distance with a long lens: two men at Netaji Subhas Chandra Bose International Airport, arrival hall, time-stamped four hours ago.

She turned the screen toward Bikram. He looked at it for a moment.

He said: Zoom in on the taller one's hands.

She zoomed. The taller man's hands were at his sides, relaxed. The kind of relaxed that is not the relaxed of a man who has nothing to worry about. The kind of relaxed that is a trained default — hands always visible, always still, always available.

He said: What's the caption.

She read: Georgian nationals, arrived Kolkata today. Sport and business visa. She paused. The comment thread under the photo, which had been posted to an MMA community forum two hours ago, contained several observations in multiple languages, most of which agreed that these were not people one would want to encounter in an enclosed space. One commenter had written simply: Mikheil and Dato. Another had replied: God help whoever they're here for.

Bikram sat with this for a moment.

The arena men had been local. Soft. The kitchen men had been slightly better — trained, coordinated, but ultimately outclassed by the combination of darkness and Para SF close-quarters doctrine. They were the employer's first and second response.

The men in the airport photograph were the third response. They were not the kind of people you field for a first or second response. You field them when the previous responses have provided you with information about the quality of the problem, and the quality of the problem warrants a qualitative upgrade in the solution.

He thought: I had six months between football night and tonight. Six months of physiotherapy, renewed certification, quiet evenings in a guesthouse near Ultadanga, and the slow, unsatisfying accumulation of routine. Six months in which my body has partially recovered and my reflexes have, in the privacy of various gym sessions I've been conducting on the grounds that the back allows controlled exercise if not operational deployment, partially re-sharpened.

He thought: partially is going to be the relevant word tonight.

He thought: I should have taken the desk job.

— ◦ —

The bus stopped at Phoolbagan. Two people got off. Nobody got on. The white Innova maintained its position three vehicles back.

Parveen said, at a level significantly quieter than her previous register: What are we going to do.

Bikram said: I'm going to work through the options. While I do that, I need you to tell me everything you know about Sandip Chakraborty.

She knew a considerable amount, as it turned out. Six years of covering eastern region combat sport had produced a detailed if unofficial understanding of the financial architecture underneath the official structure. Chakraborty had been backing fights for eleven years through a series of increasingly elaborate fronts. The Georgian connection was relatively new — eighteen months, coinciding with the arrival in Kolkata of a specific category of investment whose origin the financial press had been examining inconclusively since 2022. Tonight's fight had been the largest single-event exposure of that investment in the eastern region.

Bikram listened. He was also watching the Innova, watching the stops, calculating.

He said: The footage. You said it synced to cloud.

Yes.

Can you share it from there? Directly, without using your own accounts.

She thought about this. I can post it anonymously from a secondary account I maintain for sensitive sources. The platform would find it and verify it eventually but it would take time.

Post it now, he said. Everything. The corner administration. The timeline. Whatever documentation you have.

She said: If I post it now, before I file a proper story—

He said: If you don't post it now, the camera becomes the only copy and the camera is in a bag on a bus being followed by a white Innova containing at least two people who have a professional interest in that camera not existing.

She posted it.

The bus crossed into Howrah. The Innova, three vehicles back, crossed with it.

Bikram stood up. He said: When the bus stops at the Howrah station terminus, get off on the left side. Go into the station and don't come out the front. Go to platform nine, which connects to the station's western exit.

She said: What will you be doing.

He said: Buying us some time.

He moved to the front of the bus and spoke to the driver for thirty seconds. The driver looked at him with the expression of a man whose thousand-yard stare does not extend to this particular scenario. Then he looked at the denomination of currency Bikram placed on the dash. The stare recalibrated.

The bus stopped. Not at the terminus. At a traffic signal, on an arterial road, with the white Innova directly behind it.

The driver opened the rear door.

Bikram went out the back.

**Chapter Six**

**The Underpass**

*12:41 AM – 1:58 AM*

What followed on the arterial road was not, technically, a fight. It was a series of decisions made at speed in adverse conditions that happened to involve two people and resulted in one of them being in a significantly worse situation at the end than at the beginning.

The white Innova had stopped behind the bus. The driver — not Mikheil, not Dato, a local man, a driver — got out on instinct when the bus did something buses don't do at traffic signals. This gave Bikram the Innova's position and the driver's position and four seconds before anyone else in the vehicle registered and responded.

He used three of the four seconds to get to the Innova's passenger side, open the rear door, determine that the rear seat was empty, and remove the vehicle's key from a magnetic holder above the rear wheel well — the magnetic holder that vehicles of a certain operational purpose, across most geographies, tend to carry for exactly the reason Bikram was now using it.

He used the fourth second to put the key in his pocket and walk away at a pace consistent with a man going somewhere unremarkable.

The driver was still by the front of the bus.

The two men who'd been in the Innova's middle row were thirty metres behind Bikram and accelerating.

He ran.

— ◦ —

The Howrah underpass — the arterial underpass that carries the main road beneath the approach to Howrah Bridge — is a stretch of enclosed road approximately three hundred metres long, lit by fluorescent lights at intervals of twelve metres, at this hour carrying light vehicle traffic and no pedestrians, because no pedestrian who has thought about it would choose to walk through the Howrah underpass at one in the morning if alternatives existed.

The alternatives did not, currently, exist.

He went in.

The underpass has a specific acoustic quality: it amplifies everything. Footsteps become events. The traffic that does exist becomes a wall of sound. Voices, if anyone is using them, are impossible to locate with precision because the echo distributes them along the walls. He used this. He was not running in a straight line. He was moving diagonally, using the underpass's structure — the support pillars, the inset service alcoves, the gap where two sections of the original construction didn't quite meet and left a recessed space approximately forty centimetres deep — to break his visible profile.

One of the two men behind him was faster than the other. Significantly faster. The faster man made the underpass in the time that suggested either a running background or the specific cardiovascular conditioning of someone whose professional requirements include pursuit.

Bikram was not, at this point, in a position to fight. He was in a position to manage a pursuit in a confined acoustic space with one ear still partially compromised from the arena and a shoulder providing continuous editorial commentary. He managed it.

The service alcoves. He went into one, flat against the wall, and let the faster man pass him. Then he came out of the alcove behind the faster man and introduced the man to the underpass wall in a way that the man would remember for longer than he would prefer.

The second man slowed when he heard the introduction. He was good enough to slow, not bad enough to have stopped.

He said, in Hindi: Ruk. Stop.

Bikram said: I don't work for Chakraborty. I don't have a stake in tonight's outcome. I called a clean fight and I am trying to get home. Whatever he's paying you, it isn't worth what happens next if you keep going.

The man looked at his colleague, who was sitting against the underpass wall with the expression of a man revising his evening plans.

He said: It's not about the money.

Bikram said: Then what is it about.

He said: The Georgians are already in the city. If they find out we didn't finish this, it comes back to us.

This was, Bikram noted, a different category of admission than he'd been expecting. It was honest. It was also, in its honesty, worse than dishonesty would have been — because it confirmed that the Georgians were not a contingency. They were already operational.

He said: Tell me where they are.

A pause.

He said: I have no interest in your employer. I have no interest in his network or his investments or his arrangement with the Georgians. I want to get to the other side of this night. Tell me where they are and we're done.

The man looked at him. He made the assessment that people make when they're deciding whether another person is telling the truth. The assessment took ten seconds.

He said: Malik Ghat. Someone told them the journalist goes to the flower market. She has a source there. They're going to wait for her.

Parveen. Who had a source at the Howrah flower market and who Bikram had told to go to platform nine and wait, and who he did not currently know the location of.

He said: Thank you.

He left the underpass at its western end. Behind him, the two men remained in the underpass. One sitting against the wall. One standing. Neither following.

He found a cycle-rickshaw on the road outside the underpass. The driver was asleep on his vehicle in the specific way of men who work late hours and have learned to sleep in any position on any surface. Bikram woke him with the consideration of someone who knows what it costs to lose sleep you can't afford to lose and the urgency of someone who doesn't have time for the consideration.

He said: Malik Ghat flower market.

The driver looked at him — the blood on the ear, the shoulder posture, the general quality of a man who has had a difficult evening.

He said: Koto? How much?

Bikram named a number. The driver did not dispute it.

They went.

**Chapter Seven**

**Marigolds**

*1:58 AM – 3:44 AM*

The Malik Ghat Flower Market does not open. It never closes. It simply becomes, between approximately two in the morning and six, the most concentrated expression of itself — the maximum density of flowers, vendors, buyers, carts, and the specific organized chaos of commerce conducted at speed in a space that has been doing exactly this for a hundred and fifty years, in the shadow of one of the city's great bridges, beside a river that carries everything away.

It is, technically, the largest flower market in Asia.

It is, at two-thirty in the morning, a labyrinth.

The structure: covered sections of corrugated iron and old timber, open-sided, running in long parallel corridors between which the flowers are stacked — bundles of marigold, jasmine, tuberose, rose, gladioli — from floor to ceiling in places, ceiling being whatever the relevant shed's roof provides, generally between two and four metres overhead. The corridors are approximately one and a half metres wide. Narrower in places where the stacking has expanded into the passage and vendors have negotiated the expansion as a fact rather than an intrusion. The floor is permanently covered in loose petals, discarded stems, water from the morning hosing-down, and the accumulated biomatter of flowers in various stages of decomposition — a surface that manages to be simultaneously beautiful and utterly lethal. You cannot run on the Malik Ghat floor without expecting to fall. You cannot stand still on it without concentration. It is, in the specific physics of combat, a great equalizer.

The smell is the other thing. Jasmine and marigold and tuberose and rose and wet earth and river air and something underneath all of it that is the smell of things that are alive and in the process of becoming something else. You could get drunk on it if you stood still long enough.

Nobody was standing still.

— ◦ —

Bikram arrived on the market's eastern approach at two-twelve AM. He paid the driver, who looked at him and then at the market and said nothing, which was the appropriate response to this specific combination of circumstances.

He went in.

Parveen was not at the eastern approach. She was not at the two vendor rows closest to the road. He moved deeper, reading the market's geography — the corridor system, the central open section where the wholesale exchanges happened, the covered section to the north where the temple flower specialists worked — and found her in the north section, beside a vendor who was building a garland of marigolds with the focused speed of someone who has built several thousand garlands and has reduced the operation to pure reflex.

She looked at him with the expression of someone who had been waiting and had passed through several emotional states during the wait and had arrived at a place somewhere between relief and controlled fury.

She said: I had three missed calls from a number I didn't recognise.

He said: That's them. Did you pick up.

No.

Good. They don't know your exact position in the market. They know you come here. They don't know the layout.

She said: How do you know the layout.

He said: I walked in from the east and counted.

She looked at him. She had, Bikram noted, stopped asking the category of question that begins with who are you, because the information gathered across the last four hours had provided sufficient context to render the question technically answerable and the answer more unsettling than the question.

She said: What do we do.

He said: You're going to go to the south exit. It comes out on the ghat road, which runs along the river. There are food stalls on the ghat road at this hour. You're going to sit at a stall, order tea, and call your editor.

She said: And you.

He said: I'm going to deal with the Georgians.

A pause.

She said: That sounds like something that should be preceded by calling the police.

He said: It should be. The police response time to this address at this hour is approximately twenty-two minutes based on the regional call log averages I'm aware of. I don't have twenty-two minutes. You go south. Keep the camera.

She went south. He watched her until she was past the second row of marigold stacks and moving with purpose. Then he turned north.

— ◦ —

Mikheil Beridze was six feet two and had the build of someone who had started as a wrestler and had since redistributed the wrestling mass into something more efficient and more versatile. He moved with the specific quality of large men who have learned to be economical — no wasted motion, no broadcast of intent, the footstep placed with care. He had been in the market for eleven minutes and had already mapped the north and east sections, which told Bikram, observing him from a position behind a stack of tuberose bundles approximately fifteen metres away, that the man was doing exactly what Bikram had done: reading the space.

Dato Kvaratskhelia was smaller. Five-ten, compact, the kind of compact that is not a reduction but a concentration. He had gone west. Bikram could not currently see him. This was the more immediate problem.

A two-person clearance in a labyrinth operates on a pincer principle. One person drives from one direction while the other waits at the likely exit. The driver's job is not necessarily to engage — it's to move the target toward the other person. It's elegant. It works consistently against targets who don't understand what's happening to them.

Bikram understood what was happening to him.

The market's central open section was not viable — too exposed, no cover, the vendors there would be in the line of whatever happened. The north covered section, where the temple flower specialists worked, was better: narrower, higher stacks, the vendors working in focused pairs who could be warned and cleared without significant disruption.

He needed to separate them. A two-person pincer against a single target who knows it's a pincer is manageable only if the single target can prevent both arms of the pincer from closing simultaneously. The way you prevent this is by introducing time into the equation — dealing with one before the other arrives.

He moved north.

— ◦ —

The marigold garlands in the temple section are a different product from the bundles sold to the wholesale buyers. They are constructed with specific care — each flower placed precisely, the garland built to a standard length, eight feet, which is the correct length for draping across the image of a deity or across a door frame for a ceremony or across the chest of a body being prepared for cremation. They are built in great quantities throughout the night. They hang, finished, from every available horizontal surface — the timber ceiling beams, the metal support poles, the hooks that vendors have attached to every wall and partition over the course of decades. The north section, at two-thirty in the morning, has approximately four hundred finished garlands hanging at heights between two and six feet from the floor.

This is approximately four hundred eight-foot-length strong-woven flower cordage.

Bikram noted this with the empirical appreciation of someone assessing available resources.

He came up on Mikheil from the east, through a gap in the tuberose stacks that the geography made possible because the geography was, at this particular angle of approach, not visible from where Mikheil had positioned himself. The temple section vendor — a small man in his sixties, building garlands with the meditative efficiency of someone for whom this is the closest thing he has to prayer — saw Bikram coming, saw the direction of his attention, and without being asked, moved himself and his materials three metres to the left with the practised adaptability of someone who has been working this market for forty years and has seen most things.

Bikram took a garland off the nearest hook. Eight feet. Marigolds, tightly woven, the kind of construction that doesn't come apart under load.

Mikheil, four metres away, turned at the sound of the hook.

What followed was not the kind of combat that appears in films, which is to say it was not fluid or beautiful or choreographed into set pieces with adequate spacing for the camera. It was compressed and ugly and conducted at a range so close that the flowers were everywhere — petals releasing in clouds with each impact, the garland serving its unconventional purpose with a reliability that its makers had not designed it for — and it lasted longer than either party would have preferred.

Mikheil was better than anyone Bikram had encountered tonight. He was better in the way that changes the tactical calculation. Every previous engagement had involved people who were trained but not at the level of someone who does this professionally, repeatedly, against people who are also doing it professionally. Mikheil was the other category. He hit correctly. He covered correctly. He adapted after the first exchange with the speed of someone whose nervous system processes threat information rapidly and without the delay that most people's nervous systems insert.

Bikram's shoulder, from the arena, was the problem. It limited his left-side reach and he could feel Mikheil identifying the limitation within the first thirty seconds, targeting it, restructuring his attack geometry around it. This was, Bikram noted with the detached analytical capacity that the Para SF had developed in him precisely for moments like this, what a professional does. You find the compromise and you work it.

He let Mikheil work it. He gave him the left side, absorbed what came to the left side — and there was significant incoming — and used the right. The garland, which he'd maintained in his right hand throughout, wrapped around Mikheil's left ankle in the thirty-eighth second of the engagement and Bikram pulled.

The marigold floor did the rest.

A man of Mikheil's mass, on wet petals, ankle wrapped, going from vertical to horizontal, generates a significant impact with the ground. The significant impact with the ground had the effect that significant impacts have: a pause. A brief but operationally relevant pause.

Bikram used it.

An arm lock. The same one he'd used in the cold room, the same one that the Para SF teaches because it works across a wide range of size differentials and requires no additional equipment and produces compliance without requiring the application of force that causes injury beyond the immediate. Mikheil in the lock, on the floor, face-down in approximately three inches of marigold petals, was not going anywhere.

He said, in English: Stay there.

Mikheil said something in Georgian.

Bikram said: I'll take that as agreement.

— ◦ —

The problem was Dato.

Dato arrived from the west at the moment Bikram had Mikheil secured, which was exactly the timing the pincer was designed to produce. He arrived with the specific quality of someone who has assessed the situation in the available milliseconds and concluded that his partner is incapacitated and the target is currently occupied with the incapacitation and this is the moment.

He was correct on all three counts.

What he had not accounted for was the floor.

The Malik Ghat floor, in the north temple section at two-forty-three in the morning, after a full night of traffic and rain and flower debris and a garland that had been violently deployed, was not a surface that rewarded rapid approach from an unfamiliar angle. Dato was fast. Dato was technically excellent. Dato came in at speed from the west through a corridor of hanging jasmine garlands — the jasmine section, adjacent to the marigold, where the white flowers are built into bracelets and small wreaths — and caught his leading foot on a six-inch section of tuberose stem that was lying across the floor in exactly the position and orientation to engage with a right foot at high speed in the specific way that physics requires it to engage.

He went down.

Not all the way down. One knee, both hands to the floor, a controlled drop — this was a man whose proprioception was trained to the degree that a partial fall was processed and partially corrected before it was fully underway. But a knee to the Malik Ghat floor is a knee in flower debris and wet stone and the specific cold that stone accumulates beside a river at three in the morning, and the correction took a second and a half.

In combat, a second and a half is a significant duration.

Bikram was on him before the second and a half had elapsed. This was not a thing he was proud of — engaging a man at a momentary disadvantage is not the encounter anyone would choose. But pride and choice were not the operational considerations available at this moment, and Para SF doctrine is direct about the relationship between available considerations and chosen action: you act with what you have, when you have it.

What Bikram had was a second and a half, a right arm that was functional, and the specific knowledge that Dato was the better fighter of the two and that a second and a half was all he was going to get.

He got enough out of it. Not clean — nothing in this night had been clean — but enough. Dato was down. Not out; Bikram had not hit him hard enough or long enough to produce unconsciousness, and he did not intend to. Dato was on his back in the marigolds with the arm lock applied and the specific quality of stillness that the arm lock produces in people who understand joint mechanics.

The north temple section was quiet.

The temple flower vendor, who had moved his materials three metres to the left and had continued building garlands throughout the preceding several minutes with the focused serenity of someone who has made a professional decision to concentrate on what he can control, looked up. He looked at the two large men on his floor. He looked at Bikram. He looked at the quantity of marigold petals that had been redistributed across his workspace.

He said, in Bengali: Finished?

Bikram said: Yes.

The vendor nodded. He went back to his garlands.

— ◦ —

From approximately four metres away, a phone's camera light was on.

A young flower vendor, approximately nineteen, was filming. He had been filming for the last several minutes. He was filming with the expression of someone who cannot believe what they are witnessing but is sufficiently composed to document it, which is the specific expression of a generation that has learned to process experience through a screen.

Parveen, who had not gone south, had come north instead, and had positioned herself behind a stack of rose bundles where she was simultaneously protected and had clear sightlines, was filming on her professional camera with the composed efficiency of six years of exactly this.

Bikram looked at the camera. Looked at Parveen.

He said: I told you to go south.

She said: You told me to keep the camera. You didn't specify which direction to point it.

A pause.

He said: Fair.

He looked at the two men on the floor. Mikheil was motionless — not unconscious, thinking. Dato was watching Bikram with the specific quality of attention that a professional gives to someone they have decided to update their assessment of.

Bikram straightened up. He looked at both of them.

He said, clearly, in English — English being the language he calculated both men were more likely to share with him than Bengali: The footage from tonight's final has been posted publicly. The foul is documented. The result stands. Chakraborty's operation has a problem that is considerably bigger than me, and addressing me specifically does not reduce that problem. I am a referee. I called a clean fight. That is the entirety of my involvement.

A silence. The market moved around them. Someone three rows away was conducting a rapid transaction in a language Bikram couldn't identify. A cart went past the entrance to the north section, piled with jasmine.

Mikheil said, in English that carried the specific cadence of someone who learned it as a third language: The result does not stand. The result never stands when enough money says it does not stand.

Bikram looked at him.

He said: Then that's a problem for the people with the money.

He put both men in positions that would take them approximately four minutes to extract themselves from — not harmful, not permanent, a matter of leverage and geometry — and he walked south through the Malik Ghat, through the marigolds and jasmine and tuberose and rose, through the corridors of hanging garlands and the vendors who moved aside for him and then moved back and continued their commerce, through the market that had been doing this for a hundred and fifty years and would continue after tonight as it had continued after every other night.

At the south exit, the ghat road. The river beyond it.

The sky above Howrah, for the second time in six months, doing what Kolkata skies do at this hour: lightening at the edges. The specific pre-dawn colour that is not yet the colour of anything but is the first suggestion that colour is available.

Parveen fell into step beside him.

She said: That was. She stopped. She said: I don't have the vocabulary.

He said: Good footage?

She said: It's going to be on every platform in the country by seven AM.

He said: Good.

She said: You're bleeding again.

He said: I'm aware.

*No contest, he thought. To nobody in particular. To the marigolds.*

**Chapter Eight**

**Dead Stop**

*3:44 AM – 5:30 AM*

The tea stall on the ghat road opened at three-thirty. The man who ran it looked at Bikram and Parveen the way people who run tea stalls on ghat roads at three-thirty in the morning look at people who arrive at their stall with blood on them and flowers in their hair — which is to say, without any particular surprise and with a professional focus on the preparation of tea.

Parveen filed her story. The footage she'd posted anonymously two hours ago had already been picked up by three national sports platforms and one wire service. Her own platform's story, with her byline and her additional documentation, posted at four-twelve AM. Her editor called at four-fourteen and said several things in rapid succession that included the word extraordinary four times and the phrase potential liability twice and concluded with the clear implication that both of them understood that the story was the story and the liability was the liability and they would address the liability later.

Bikram drank his tea. He watched the river. At this hour the Hooghly was beginning to carry the first light — not sunrise, something earlier, the light that arrives before the sun as a preliminary report on conditions.

He thought about Rajan Mitra, who was in Shibpur at his uncle's, with a championship he had won honestly in a fight that had been set up for him to lose honestly. The championship would stand. The documentation made it irrefutable. Rajan's career, which had been constructed on the premise of fair competition, was beginning from a position of documented and publicly verified fairness. This was, in the context of the eastern region's combat sport ecosystem, unusual.

He thought about the two men in the marigolds, who were by now extracted and ambulatory and making whatever calls they needed to make to whatever people needed to be called. He did not think about them with particular concern. They were professionals. Professionals recalibrate.

He thought about Chakraborty, who had a problem that was not going to be solved by the night's events and was, in fact, materially worse than it had been at nine-forty PM. The footage was public. The AIFSFO's formal review would begin in the morning. The regional crime press would have the syndicate connection by afternoon. This was not Bikram's problem to solve. It was a problem for the people whose job it was to solve it, most of whom he had no confidence in but several of whom, specifically the ones in the national sports integrity unit who had been building a case on Chakraborty for fourteen months, were, based on the available evidence, actually doing their jobs.

He thought about the optometrist in Ultadanga, whose report had been the beginning of all of this. The original report, from six months ago, had disintegrated in his pocket. He had a new report, from a month ago, from a different optometrist, with a different prognosis — the nerve cluster, contrary to the initial assessment, had not continued to degrade. It had stabilized. It might, with specific therapeutic intervention, partially recover. The new optometrist had said this with the careful qualification of a professional who does not wish to offer encouragement that the data doesn't support, and the careful qualification had contained enough encouragement that Bikram had renewed his certification.

He still wasn't entirely sure why.

— ◦ —

Parveen put her phone down. She looked at the river. After a moment she said: Can I ask you something.

He said: Yes.

She said: You could have left the venue after the call. Before they mobilized. Before any of it. You had a window.

He said: I know.

She said: But you went back into the arena.

He said: There was a situation.

She said: There was a situation that you chose to engage with rather than walk away from.

A pause. The river.

He said: I called a fair fight. The call had consequences. Walking away from the consequences doesn't undo the call. It just means someone else deals with the consequences, probably someone less equipped to deal with them than I am.

She looked at him. She said: That's a very specific philosophy.

He said: It's not a philosophy. It's just how the arithmetic works.

She said: Do you referee often?

He said: Often enough.

She said: Why?

He sat with the question. The Hooghly was doing what the Hooghly does: moving. The first boats of the day were beginning to appear — the small ferry that crosses to the Howrah side at five, its light visible at the ghat's far end.

He said: A retired schoolteacher once told me that even wrong men come to the match. I think about that.

She said: And you think you're a wrong man in the right seat now? Or the right man in a wrong seat?

He said: I think those categories are less fixed than they seem. I think what you're equipped for changes, if you work at it.

A silence. Not uncomfortable.

She said: You should get that ear looked at.

He said: I will.

She said: There's a clinic near the station that opens at six.

He said: I know. I walked past it on football night.

She looked at him. She said: You're the football referee. The one from six months ago.

He said: Guilty.

She said: I covered that story. I thought you disappeared.

He said: I did. Then I came back.

She looked at him for a long moment. Then she looked at the river. She said: I think you should let me write a story about you.

He said: Absolutely not.

She said: The sport official who gets hunted twice in six months by two completely different organized—

He said: No.

She said: It's a good story.

He said: I understand that it's a good story. The answer is still no.

She smiled. It was the first time he'd seen her smile. The evening had not previously provided occasions for it.

She said: Okay.

— ◦ —

At five-fourteen AM, his phone rang.

Colonel Ghosh.

He answered.

Ghosh said: I've been trying to reach you since midnight. What happened at that arena?

Bikram said: I made a call. There were consequences.

A silence. Then: Are you alright?

He said: More or less.

Ghosh said: The AIFSFO chief official filed an incident report. The footage is everywhere. The syndicate connection is being confirmed. He paused. You'll need to give a statement.

He said: I know.

Ghosh said: Bikram. There's a national-level appointment. Multi-sport. Part of the integrity initiative they're rolling out after the Delhi case. They need senior officials with clean records and verifiable independence. The review board asked me if I knew anyone.

A long pause.

Bikram looked at the river. The light was properly arriving now — not just suggested but present, the sky making its colour commitment in the east, the Hooghly going from dark to something that had gold in it. Beside him, Parveen was writing something in a notebook, having apparently decided that the recorder was not what this moment required.

He said: I'll call you back.

He didn't hang up. He sat with the line open, the call timer running.

He thought about the marigolds. The garland in his hand. The floor. The market continuing around the violence as though the violence were a weather event — present, acknowledged, then absorbed and superseded by the next transaction.

He thought about the vendor who had moved three metres to the left and continued building garlands and when it was over had looked up and said: Finished?

He thought: yes. No. Not particularly.

He said, into the phone: Send me the details.

He hung up. He drank the last of his tea. He looked at his officiating credentials, which were still in his wallet, which had been in his pocket through everything.

He thought: I am a referee.

He thought: this is a strange thing to be.

He thought: and yet.

**Epilogue: The Rule Book**

The AIFSFO formally upheld the result of the Kolkata Combat Championship final at its emergency review board meeting, eleven days after the event. The corner foul was confirmed. The disqualification of Viktor Kasparov's corner was confirmed. Rajan Mitra remained champion.

The review board also issued a formal commendation to the officiating referee for correct application of the relevant rule under adversarial conditions. The commendation used the phrase conspicuous professional integrity, which is the language the review board uses when it means: he did the right thing and it cost him considerably, and the board would like to acknowledge this without examining too closely the nature of the cost.

Sandip Chakraborty was arrested fourteen days after the event on charges related to match-fixing and criminal conspiracy. The national sports integrity unit, which had been building its case for fourteen months, found that the footage and the subsequent documentation provided by Parveen Siddiqui's reporting had significantly accelerated the timeline. Her story ran in four parts over six weeks and was nominated for a national sports journalism award, the result of which was not yet known at the time this account was compiled.

Mikheil Beridze and Dato Kvaratskhelia departed Kolkata on a flight to Tbilisi two days after the event at the flower market. The regional crime press noted their departure with interest. No charges were filed. They did not return.

Rajan Mitra went on to compete in the national championship three months later. He won. His corner was clean.

Bikram Dey took the national integrity official appointment. His first assignment was a wrestling tournament in Lucknow. The second was a boxing qualifier in Chennai. The third was a martial arts event in Guwahati, which was close to Manipur, close to the terrain he'd once operated in, and which he found, to his own quiet surprise, felt like a different kind of return.

He kept the AIFSFO credentials in his wallet. He kept the optometrist's latest report in his jacket pocket. He did not keep it folded into quarters. He did not intend to let it disintegrate.

The rule book for combat sport officiating runs to sixty-three pages. Page forty-one contains the following:

*A referee must be willing to make an unpopular decision in the presence of a hostile crowd, financial pressure, or personal risk. The legitimacy of the sport depends entirely on the legitimacy of the official. Without this, there is no contest.*

Bikram had read this on the night before his first combat officiating assignment, in the same guesthouse in Ultadanga, in the same room, at the same desk. He had read it and thought about the football night, about the wrong calls and the lanes and the bridge and the dawn in Howrah, and then he had put the rule book down and gone to sleep.

He read it again after the flower market.

He thought it was still true. He thought it was the truest thing the federation had written, despite the federation's many shortcomings, of which he had intimate and ongoing knowledge.

He put the rule book down.

He went to sleep.
