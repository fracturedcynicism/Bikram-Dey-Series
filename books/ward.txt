WARD

A Novella

Being the fifth night of Bikram Dey

FRACTURED CYNICISM

Ward (n.):
1. A division of a hospital or institution.
2. To ward off: to deflect, to keep at a distance.
3. A person under the care or protection of another.
4. A division. A separation. The act of being partitioned.

---

Three books ago, a man named Bikram Dey made three wrong calls at a football match.

He survived.

Two books ago, he made one correct call at a combat tournament.

He survived.

One book ago, he protected an election and investigated an institutional conspiracy.

He survived. Barely. His body filed comprehensive dissenting opinions.

This story is about what surviving cost.

It takes place over thirty-nine hours.

He checks in on a Tuesday.

By Thursday morning either he leaves or he doesn't.

The word for the condition between those two facts is the title.

**CHAPTER ONE**

**The Intake**

*Maitri Wellness and Rehabilitation Centre, Barrackpore — Tuesday, 3:00 PM*

He had agreed to come voluntarily. This was the fact he kept in front of everything else like a man carrying something fragile through a crowd — held out, kept clear, not allowed to touch anything. He had agreed. He had made the appointment himself. He had taken the auto from Ultadanga to Sealdah and the train from Sealdah to Barrackpore and the facility's pickup vehicle from the station, and at no point in any of that sequence had anyone compelled him to be there. He had decided. The decision was his.

He told himself this at regular intervals on the way and understood, in the part of him that had been trained to identify the difference between orientation and fabrication, that the regularity of the telling was itself information.

The facility's intake process took forty-seven minutes. Forms, photographs, a brief assessment with the duty nurse, the surrender of his phone to the storage locker — standard procedure, voluntary patients could access personal items during scheduled periods, the nurse explained this with the specific warmth of someone who has explained it many times and has not yet stopped meaning it. His wallet. His keys. His officiating credentials, which he kept in his jacket pocket always and which the nurse looked at briefly and did not comment on.

The fourth optometrist's report. He kept that too. The nurse did not ask about it.

He was shown to Room 7, Ward A, first floor. Standard single room. A bed, a chair, a window overlooking the garden, a bathroom with a door that locked from the inside which the nurse confirmed twice, the confirmation itself a form of institutional reassurance that told him something about what previous patients had needed to be reassured about.

He understood the implications.

The garden was exactly what the brochure had said. He had read the brochure three times in the weeks before admission, not because he doubted it but because reading it was something to do while he was not making the appointment. The garden: maintained, planted with things that were chosen for their softness, their non-sharpness, their capacity to receive a person sitting on a bench at any hour without demanding anything back. It was a garden that had been designed by someone who understood what a garden needed to be for the people who would use it.

He stood at the window and looked at it.

Then he sat on the edge of the bed and looked at his hands.

— ◦ —

He thought: I am here because the physiotherapist said I was ready for light officiating duties and the physiotherapist was right but light officiating duties had, in the subsequent eight months, accumulated a specific gravitational pull that light did not fully describe. And the accumulation had produced a set of conditions that multiple people — the physiotherapist, the optometrist, the AIFSFO's medical panel, and finally Brigadier Meenakshi Nair — had identified with the same word.

The word was: rest.

He had heard the word four times from four separate sources before he made the appointment.

He had needed to hear it four times.

But he also understood, in the specific way that comes from two books of living inside institutional frameworks that were designed to contain him, that rest had many definitions.

The clinical definition: reduced physiological and psychological stress, allowing the body's healing mechanisms to function.

The institutional definition: removal of the active variable from the equation.

He had not told anyone which definition he thought the AIFSFO's medical panel had meant.

— ◦ —

The first evening: dinner in the common dining room. Six other patients from Ward A, two of whom he would come to know and four of whom remained at the necessary distance that institutions produce between people who are all managing something and don't have the bandwidth for additional management.

Dr. Sunita Arora introduced herself after dinner. She was smaller than he had expected from her voice on the phone — the initial consultations had been by call — with the specific quality of attention that distinguishes a doctor who is genuinely looking at you from one who is performing the act of looking. She had reviewed his case notes. Not just the recent ones. All of them.

She had the file from the AIFSFO's medical panel. She had the file from the optometrist. She had, because the AIFSFO's insurance had required it and he had authorized it, the file from the election commission.

She said: I've read everything. The election night incident. The assessment that followed. The documentation of what you identified as systematic interference and what the commission concluded was elevated threat perception.

He looked at her.

She said: It's in the file as pattern-matching bias based on your operational background.

He said: Yes.

She said: But the election was fair. The interference was prevented. Your actions were operationally correct.

He said: Yes.

She said: And they documented you as having potentially compromised judgment while acknowledging that your judgment was correct.

A pause. He said: Yes.

She looked at him. Not the clinical look — something adjacent to it. The look of a doctor who has read everything and is integrating what a chart is and what a person is and watching for the gap.

She said: Under the Mental Healthcare Act, voluntary admission means you retain the right to request discharge at any time. I want you to know that explicitly.

He said: I know the Act.

She said: I know you do. I'm telling you anyway. Because I tell everyone anyway.

He said: I appreciate it.

She said: The programme is twenty-one days. Physiotherapy every morning, occupational therapy in the afternoons, psychiatric support twice a week. The pharmacological protocol begins tomorrow — it's primarily for sleep and acute pain management. Nothing you haven't been prescribed before.

He said: Who reviews the pharmacological protocol.

She said: I do. Personally. Every three days.

He said: And the data from those reviews. Where does it go.

She looked at him. A recalibration of the looking.

She said: Our standard data governance protocol is HIPAA-equivalent. Patient data is anonymised for aggregate clinical outcomes research, which is disclosed in the admission documents you signed.

He said: Who is the research partner.

A pause. Short. He noted it.

She said: Clearpath Wellness Analytics. They're a data optimisation company. The arrangement is standard in the industry.

She said it the way a person says something that they believe and have never examined.

But he had examined it. Two months ago, sitting in a basement in Howrah with Dr. Rajan Ghosh, he had understood the shape of the Hastings Circle — the institutional machinery that had been arranged to produce a specific outcome. He had traced Commodore Mitra. He had documented the DRDO turbine programme. He had identified Subramaniam's network.

And he had, in the process, made himself visible to that network.

He had stepped back from the Hastings investigation — not fully, never fully, but into a space where the visible threat had been deferred. He had told himself it was because the evidence was with Meenakshi Nair and the data was with authorities and the investigation had moved beyond his personal remit.

The true reason was simpler: his body was at forty-five percent and his credibility was asterisked by an incident report that documented him as potentially biased and the next investigation would cost him more than the last one and he was running out of body to spend.

So he had come here.

Not to rest. To be removed from the operational field. To be placed — through his own agreement, which was the crucial detail — in an institutional setting where he could not continue the work.

Which meant that the person who placed him here did not need to place him here by force.

He said: Thank you. That's all I needed to know.

She said it the way a person says something that they believe and have never examined.

She said: Goodnight, Mr. Dey. The medication will be with you at nine.

After she left he sat in Room 7 and looked at the window and the garden beyond it and the dark that had settled over the garden, and thought about what Kashyap would be doing right now. What Kashyap had done two months ago, when the ministry review had been initiated, when Subramaniam's network had started showing strain, when the Hastings Circle had begun the process of calculating what could be saved and what would need to be abandoned.

What Kashyap had done was: nothing visible. The AIFSFO's medical panel had convened. The panel consisted of genuine doctors with genuine credentials. They had reviewed the available data and made their recommendation based on legitimate clinical criteria. What they had not been told, what they had not needed to be told because the system was elegant enough to work without explicit instruction, was that one specific data point — the election commission's incident report, the one documenting pattern-matching bias — had been weighted more heavily in their assessment than others.

The incident report existed.

The incident report documented that he was right about the threat and questionable in his judgment about the threat.

Both things were true. Both things were in the file.

The medical panel had simply read the file. They had done their job. And the output of their job, properly interpreted by someone who understood what the output was designed to do, was removal.

He said to the nurse at nine PM: I'm ready for the medication.

She brought it. He took it. It tasted like every sleep medication ever tasted — chemical, specific, engineered for a particular purpose.

He went to bed.

He slept from ten until three-twelve AM.

Then he woke up and the ceiling of Room 7 was the ceiling and nothing else and he lay in the dark and began his inventory.

He lay in the dark until four AM. Then he got up and walked to the window and looked at the garden. The garden at four in the morning was the garden — benches, planted borders, the small fountain that was turned off at night, the particular darkness of a space designed to be seen in daylight and still present and patient in the dark.

His mind was running the assessment. He had walked the facility twice the previous evening — once before dinner, once after, on the grounds that a new environment requires orientation and orientation requires walking. The facility had been built in 1987. The original installation included fire doors, exit signage, a basement level with the building's technical infrastructure. He had noted the alarm systems during his walks. He had noted the access protocols. He had noted which doors had manual overrides and which required key cards.

He had noted all of this with the specific attention of someone whose job, for twelve years, had been noting available resources in unfamiliar environments.

He had also noted that noting these things, in an institutional setting dedicated to helping people stop doing this exact thing, was itself evidence of the pathology the setting was designed to treat.

This was the closed loop. This was the horror.

Not that the hypervigilance diagnosis was wrong. The hypervigilance was real. He was hypervigilant. The election night had made him hypervigilant. The Hastings Circle investigation had made him more hypervigilant. The specific knowledge that the person managing his removal had been at the centre of the machinery he had investigated made the hypervigilance completely proportional to what was actually there.

But the file did not include the knowledge about Kashyap. The file included the election commission's report. It included the AIFSFO's medical panel assessment. It included the evaluation that his threat perception, while operationally vindicated, was elevated beyond what the operational context required.

Both things simultaneously true.

Correct and compromised.

Right and unreliable.

He sat back down on the bed. He looked at his hands. He thought: I am going to need to be very careful here.

At six AM the corridor lights came on. At six-fifteen, footsteps. At six-thirty, the morning medication was delivered to each room. He watched the nurse move down the corridor through the gap under his door, counting the interval between deliveries to establish the timing.

He noted that he was counting.

He noted, again, the pattern of his own noting.

He took the morning medication. He drank the water. He watched the ceiling.

The ceiling was the ceiling. He confirmed this twice.

**CHAPTER THREE**

**Clearpath**

*Wednesday — Day One — 08:00 AM*

WEDNESDAY 08:00 AM — TIME REMAINING: 31 HOURS

The morning was structured. This was the point of structured mornings in facilities like this one — the structure was not administrative convenience, it was therapeutic scaffolding, a framework that replaced the collapsed internal rhythm of a person who has been running on adrenaline and operational necessity for too long and whose own clock has stopped keeping reliable time. Bikram understood this. He had read enough about the programme before admission to understand its logic.

He also understood that he was observing the structure rather than inhabiting it, which was the difference between a man being treated and a man processing his environment, and that this difference was itself something the chart would note if it were visible.

Physiotherapy at eight. Pradeep ran the morning session — a compact man in his early fifties, methodical, with the specific quality of a physiotherapist who has been doing this long enough that the hands know things before the mind has articulated them. He worked on the knee first. The knee, under professional attention, made the sound it made when something was being addressed that had been waiting a long time for addressing.

Pradeep said nothing for the first twenty minutes. This was, Bikram noted, a specific skill.

Then Pradeep said, without looking up from the knee: You walked the corridor twice last night.

Bikram said: How do you know that.

Pradeep said: The floor between Room 7 and the nurses' station has a specific creak at the third board. Every night-shift staff member learns which boards creak and which don't. Patients usually don't.

A pause.

Bikram said: I needed to orient.

Pradeep said: That's what people say.

He went back to the knee. His hands continued their work with the focused competence of someone whose hands are, in the truest sense, the point.

He said: How long have you been running at this level.

Bikram said: Define the level.

Pradeep said: Whatever level produces a right knee in this condition in a man of your age and fitness baseline. The cartilage has the wear pattern of someone who has been using it for burst activity under load for approximately two years without adequate recovery intervals.

Bikram said: About that.

Pradeep said: And the shoulder.

Bikram said: Impact trauma. Fourteen months ago.

Pradeep said: It was treated when.

Bikram said: It was addressed in physiotherapy six months ago.

Pradeep looked up. Addressed is a specific word.

Bikram said: It was managed in physiotherapy.

Pradeep nodded. He went back to the knee. He said: The ribs. You favor the left side. You protect the left side. Rib fractures that haven't been fully rehabilitated produce a specific compensatory motion. You've had it since you arrived. But it's worse than six months of accumulated stress would produce. Much worse. Which means you took a recent impact. Post-physiotherapy. Which means you were cleared for light activity and immediately engaged in heavy activity.

Bikram was quiet.

Pradeep said: The election night. The news covered it. National integrity official prevented election interference. Very clean story. No ongoing drama. Just a successful operation.

He said: Except operations that succeed in the way that creates institutional doubt tend to have ongoing drama. And an official who succeeds in a way that creates doubt about his judgment tends to continue investigating. Which tends to produce more operations.

Bikram said: You've been doing this for a long time.

Pradeep said: Twenty-three years. I see people who come in to rest and I see people who come in because they've been removed. Most people are just one or the other. You're both. And the second reason is never documented in the intake, because the second reason requires that someone at an institutional level has decided it should be invisible.

He finished the session. He made notes. The notes went into the system. The system was the facility's patient management software. Bikram had seen the login screen when the nurse processed his admission — the system was called MaitriCare 3.1. He had seen the interface. He had seen, in the interface's top menu bar, a small logo next to the data sync indicator.

He had seen the logo before.

He had seen it two months ago, in a basement in Howrah, in a file that had been on a terminal that should have been decommissioned. The logo: a small stylised path, a road converging to a point. Clearpath Wellness Analytics.

The same data partner Dr. Arora had mentioned.

He had known it since the intake. He had spent the night confirming what he already knew.

He was not surprised. Not surprised and alarmed are different things. He was both.

**CHAPTER FOUR**

**The Protocol**

*Wednesday — Afternoon*

The afternoon medication was different from the morning medication.

He noticed this the way he noticed things that were in the category of information — without alarm, without drama, with the methodical registration of a man who has been trained to notice what is there and classify it accurately. The morning medication: a mild sedative, a nerve pain compound, a B-complex supplement. The afternoon: the same two plus something else. A third compound. Small, white, unremarkable.

He palmed it.

The palming was automatic. A technique from a training context he hadn't used in years, developed in scenarios where the scenario required you to appear to take something you were not taking. The nurse did not watch him swallow. This was not suspicious — the facility's intake protocol noted that Ward A patients were not monitored during medication administration, as monitoring was considered inconsistent with the voluntary admission ethos. He had read this in the admission documents.

He was glad he had read the admission documents.

He took the palmed tablet to the bathroom. He looked at it. He did not have the means to identify it without a lab. What he had was the knowledge that the morning protocol had been prescribed by Dr. Arora and the afternoon protocol had been prescribed by Dr. Arora and yet the afternoon protocol contained a compound that had not appeared on the typed medication sheet the nurse had shown him on admission.

He noted this discrepancy. He filed it. He flushed the tablet.

He sat on the bed and thought about what this meant.

Dr. Arora had said: the pharmacological protocol begins tomorrow — it's primarily for sleep and acute pain management. Nothing you haven't been prescribed before.

The afternoon protocol was different from the morning protocol.

The afternoon protocol was being administered to him without being listed on his printed prescription.

Either:
A) Dr. Arora had added an afternoon compound to his protocol after intake and had not updated the printed prescription, which would be an administrative oversight but not impossible.

Or:

B) The afternoon compound was not part of Dr. Arora's prescribed protocol but was being administered anyway, which would be a violation of her authority and the consent framework.

Or:

C) Dr. Arora knew about the afternoon compound and had not disclosed it to him, which would be a deliberate omission of information required for informed consent.

The three possibilities created a gradient of institutional violation. He did not know which one was true. What he knew was that the afternoon compound was being given to him without his explicit knowledge of what it was or why it was prescribed.

He thought about the election night incident report. He thought about what it said: the official's threat perception appears elevated in response to initial intercept attempts.

He thought about Kashyap, who had said — through the mechanism of institutional machinery — that an official whose threat perception was elevated could be managed by making him see threats where they might not exist and then documenting those sightings as evidence of his unreliability.

He thought: I am lying in a bed in a facility managed by Clearpath Wellness Analytics and receiving a medication that is not on my printed prescription.

He thought: this is either a violation or I am seeing a violation where there is none.

He thought: my chart describes exactly this pattern. The seeing of violations where there might not be any.

He thought: the specific genius of it is that I cannot trust my own pattern recognition anymore, because the pattern recognition is itself the symptom.

He lay in the dark and understood, for the first time in the book, the full architecture of what had been done.

It was not that they had removed him from the field. It was that they had removed him and made the removal look voluntary and placed him in a setting where his hypervigilance — his actual, operationally vindicated, chronically accurate hypervigilance — would be read as evidence of pathology.

And the pathology was real. The hypervigilance was real. Both things true simultaneously. He had been made, through two books of being right in ways that created doubt, into exactly the kind of person who would be put in this facility.

And the facility was reading him correctly. He was hypervigilant. His threat perception was elevated. His pattern recognition was running hot. He was seeing violations — or possible violations, or the structure of possible violations — where a relaxed person might not see them.

The only error in the diagnosis was this: the elevated threat perception was not a symptom. It was correct. It was warranted. It was the appropriate response to an environment that had been specifically arranged to be threatening.

He thought: this is the Lovecraft answer. Not the thing you can fight. The system. The structure. The specific arrangement that works perfectly whether or not anyone is consciously managing it.

He did not flush the second tablet. He kept it. He was going to need to know what it was. He was going to need to understand what his chart was being prepared to call his symptom.

He kept it in his pocket like a piece of evidence. Like proof that something real had happened and was still happening.

He was also aware that keeping contraband medication in his pocket was itself evidence of the exact symptom he was trying to prove he didn't have.

**CHAPTER FIVE**

**The Decision**

*Wednesday — Afternoon Session*

His session with Dr. Arora was at three PM.

She was good at what she did. He had known this from the first evening and the afternoon session confirmed it. She listened without the performed quality of listening — no therapeutic head-tilting, no echoing, just the clean attention of a doctor who is interested in what a patient is actually saying. She asked about the previous books. Not in those terms — she didn't know them as books, she knew them as events, as the clinical presentation of cumulative operational trauma, as the documented history that the AIFSFO's medical panel had produced and that she had read with the thoroughness of someone who reads everything she is given.

She asked about the football night. He answered. He was accurate. He told her what had happened and what it had felt like and the honest answer to both was not the answer of a broken man or a frightened man but of a man who had been doing a job in conditions that exceeded what the job's description specified, and who had continued doing it, and who was here now because the continuation had a cost.

She listened. She noted. The notes went into MaitriCare 3.1. MaitriCare 3.1 synced with Clearpath.

He answered her questions and told the truth and understood that the truth was being filed in a system whose use of it he could not currently prevent.

She said, at the session's end: I want to adjust the afternoon medication.

He said: Which compound.

She looked at him. A careful look.

She said: The sleep support formulation. I think we can reduce the dosage now that—

He said: What are the full listed components.

She said: I'm sorry?

He said: I'd like to see the full listed components of the afternoon protocol. The complete prescription.

A pause. She said: I can absolutely provide that. Is there something specific—

He said: I'd like to review it. That's all.

She printed it. She handed it to him. He read it. The printed list contained three compounds — the same three he had been told about on admission. The third compound: zolpidem tartrate, five milligrams. A sleep aid. Standard. Clinically appropriate.

Not the tablet he had palmed and flushed.

Not the tablet he was carrying in his pocket.

The tablet he had palmed and flushed had been smaller and a different shape than the zolpidem tartrate that appeared on the printed prescription.

He held the printed prescription. He looked at it.

He said: Thank you.

She said: Of course. I want you to feel—

He said: I know. Thank you.

He went back to Room 7. He sat in the chair. He looked at the garden.

He thought about the specific decision point. He could ask for discharge. The Mental Healthcare Act was clear. Voluntary admission meant voluntary departure. He could request discharge and within twenty-four hours, subject to administrative review, he would have to be released unless the facility certified that he presented a danger to himself or others.

If they certified that, he could request a Mental Health Review Board hearing.

The MHRB was appointed by the district administration and conducted hearings in accordance with the Act. The hearings were legitimate. The process was legal.

But the certification was the administrator's. Rohit Chandra could certify. And once Chandra certified, the process of release became a process of legal review rather than administrative release. Months. Possibly longer.

He could also stay. Stay and cooperate with the programme and take the medications — all the medications, including the one not on the printed prescription — and let the facility do whatever it was designed to do to him.

Or he could do a third thing.

The third thing required knowledge of the building. It required understanding of the systems. It required another person. It required the capability to move a body that was at forty percent and had not slept properly in six weeks and was being given medications whose purpose was not entirely clear.

The third thing was the option that had landed him here in the first place. The refusal to accept the system as final. The insistence on seeing what was actually there and acting on it.

He thought: I have two days. I have a body at forty percent. I have the knowledge that Kashyap has already arranged something and that something is in motion and that the medication I'm being given is part of what's in motion.

He thought: Meenakshi Nair would tell me to stay. To cooperate. To let the process happen. To wait for the legal review.

He thought: but Meenakshi Nair is not here. I am here. And the thing I do — the thing that has brought me to every night of this book — is I see what is there and I call it.

He looked at the clock. He had not been given his phone but the room had a wall clock — institutional, round-faced, the kind of clock that has been keeping time in institutional rooms since before anyone in the room was born.

Wednesday. Four-seventeen PM.

WEDNESDAY 04:17 PM — TIME REMAINING: 22 HOURS 43 MINUTES

He thought: I am going to find Meera. She was the architect. She understands systems. She understands what Clearpath is doing because she built part of what Clearpath does. And she has been here nineteen days and she will have seen things I have not seen.

He stood up. He looked at the garden one more time.

Then he went to find the person in Room 4.

Chapter Seven
Room Fourteen
Thursday — 5:00 AM
Room 14's patient was named Devraj Nair. Forty-one years old. He had been a data protection lawyer. Specialising in regulatory compliance for healthcare data companies. His practice had included representing, for eighteen months, several whistleblowers in cases involving health data misuse. He had been one of the people Meera had consulted, not as a client but as a contact, when she had first understood what the Clearpath pipeline was doing.
Six weeks after their first conversation, Nair had a breakdown. This was the clinical description. In the description was the word breakdown and the word voluntary and the word Maitri.
He had been here seven months.
His RTI applications — filed through a legal aid organisation before his admission, and continuing through a paralegal contact who managed the filings — were three of the same ones that had been rejected or left pending in the cases Bikram had reviewed during the Glass Rulebook research. The same institutional architecture. Different players. Same mechanism.

He is a man with a compromised left shoulder, a right knee that files regular correspondence, three lumbar vertebrae with institutional memory, a pair of eyes that are recovering at the margin, and a reflex — old, deep, Para SF and older — that sees the gap in a chant, the shoulder not the hand, the face in a crowd, the tablet that doesn't match the prescription, and calls it.
That's it. That's the whole thing. Everything else is consequence.
He looked at the canvas. He looked at the fighters coming out of their corners.
He walked to the centre and watched the hands.
